# CHOLERA: COMPREHENSIVE OUTBREAK INTELLIGENCE REPORT
Source: World Health Organization (WHO), CDC, International Centre for Diarrhoeal Disease Research Bangladesh (icddr,b)

# Overview
Cholera is an acute diarrheal disease caused by infection of the intestine with toxigenic Vibrio cholerae bacteria, primarily serogroups O1 and O139. It is characterized by profuse, watery diarrhea (classically described as "rice-water stools") that can rapidly lead to severe dehydration and death if untreated. Cholera is fundamentally a disease of inadequate water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) infrastructure and disproportionately affects populations living in poverty, conflict zones, and areas affected by natural disasters. With prompt and appropriate oral rehydration therapy, the case fatality rate can be reduced from above 25 percent (without treatment) to below 1 percent. Cholera is endemic in many countries across Asia, Africa, and Latin America, and continues to cause large outbreaks, particularly in humanitarian crisis settings.

# Outbreak History
Cholera has caused seven major global pandemics since the 19th century and continues to cause the ongoing seventh pandemic. The disease has profoundly shaped human history, driving the development of modern public health infrastructure, epidemiology, and sanitation systems.

The First through Sixth Pandemics (1817-1923): The first pandemic originated in India in 1817 and spread across Asia. Subsequent pandemics followed throughout the 19th and early 20th centuries, reaching Europe, the Americas, and beyond. The third pandemic (1852-1860) was the deadliest, killing over one million people in Russia alone. During the third pandemic, British physician John Snow famously traced a cholera outbreak in London to a contaminated water pump on Broad Street — a landmark event in the history of epidemiology that established the waterborne transmission of cholera and founded the discipline of modern epidemiology.

The Seventh Pandemic (1961-Present): The seventh pandemic, caused by the El Tor biotype of V. cholerae O1, began in Indonesia in 1961 and has never been declared over. It has spread to virtually every continent and continues to cause endemic disease and outbreaks in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. A new O139 serogroup emerged in Bangladesh and India in 1992, initially feared to be the start of an eighth pandemic, but O1 El Tor has remained the dominant global strain. Major cholera outbreaks in recent decades include: Peru 1991 (over 320,000 cases, first cholera in the Americas in the 20th century), Zimbabwe 2008-2009 (over 98,000 cases, 4,200 deaths), Haiti 2010 (over 800,000 cases, approximately 10,000 deaths — introduced following the devastating earthquake by UN peacekeepers from Nepal), Yemen 2016-present (the largest cholera outbreak in recorded history with over 2.5 million cases and 3,500 deaths by 2019), and ongoing outbreaks across 25+ countries in 2022-2023 representing the worst global cholera situation in decades.

The Global Cholera Resurgence 2021-2024: The world is experiencing an unprecedented cholera crisis, with a dramatic resurgence in cases and geographic spread. In 2022, cholera cases reported to WHO nearly doubled compared to 2021, with over 473,000 cases and 2,349 deaths reported from 44 countries. The countries most affected include Haiti, Cameroon, Syria, Nigeria, Ethiopia, Somalia, Malawi, Mozambique, and many others. The resurgence is driven by climate change (floods and droughts destroying water infrastructure), conflict and political instability, population displacement, underfunding of WASH infrastructure, and a severe global shortage of oral cholera vaccines. Cholera in 2023-2024 has reached levels not seen in decades, prompting WHO to declare a global crisis.

# Global Statistics
WHO estimates that cholera causes 1.3 to 4 million cases and 21,000 to 143,000 deaths per year worldwide. The enormous range in these estimates reflects substantial underreporting — many cases are not diagnosed and many countries do not report cholera data to WHO due to political and economic considerations (cholera outbreaks can damage tourism and trade). The true burden is estimated to be 3 to 5 times higher than officially reported figures. Approximately 47 countries are considered endemic for cholera. Sub-Saharan Africa bears the greatest burden, accounting for approximately 73 percent of all cholera cases reported to WHO in recent years. Southern Asia, particularly Bangladesh, India, and Pakistan, has long-standing endemic cholera. Latin America experienced significant cholera burden in the 1990s following the seventh pandemic introduction and again in Haiti after 2010.

# Transmission
Cholera is transmitted through ingestion of water or food contaminated with V. cholerae O1 or O139 bacteria. Contamination typically occurs through fecal contamination of water sources or food. The organism survives in aquatic environments, particularly in brackish coastal and estuarine waters, where it can exist in a dormant state associated with plankton. Human-to-human transmission through direct contact is rare; the primary route is waterborne transmission via contaminated drinking water. Foodborne transmission can occur through raw or undercooked seafood (particularly shellfish harvested from contaminated waters), raw fruits and vegetables irrigated with contaminated water, and foods contaminated during preparation by infected food handlers. The infectious dose of V. cholerae is relatively high (10^6 to 10^11 organisms for healthy adults), meaning that the risk of infection from low-level contamination is limited; however, this dose can be achieved readily in heavily contaminated water sources. Gastric acid is an important host defense — individuals with reduced gastric acidity (due to antacid use or prior gastric surgery) are more susceptible to infection.

# Symptoms
The spectrum of cholera illness ranges from asymptomatic infection (estimated 75 percent of all infections in endemic settings) to mild or moderate diarrhea to the severe life-threatening syndrome of cholera gravis. The incubation period is very short — typically 12 hours to 5 days (most commonly 1-3 days) — reflecting rapid bacterial colonization and toxin production in the small intestine. Cholera toxin (CT) produced by V. cholerae stimulates adenylate cyclase in intestinal epithelial cells, leading to massive secretion of chloride and water into the intestinal lumen and producing the characteristic profuse watery diarrhea.

Cholera Gravis: In its most severe form, cholera begins abruptly with sudden onset of profuse watery diarrhea (up to 1 liter per hour) and vomiting. The stool has a characteristic "rice-water" appearance — pale, cloudy water with white flecks of mucus — and a fishy odor. This extreme fluid loss (up to 10-20 liters per day) can lead to profound dehydration within hours. Clinical signs of severe dehydration include extreme thirst, decreased skin turgor (skin does not snap back after pinching), sunken eyes, dry mouth and mucous membranes, no urine output, muscle cramps (due to electrolyte imbalances, particularly hypocalcemia), weak pulse, low blood pressure, and in severe cases, hypovolemic shock, metabolic acidosis, and death. Without treatment, severe cholera can kill within hours of symptom onset.

# Case Fatality Rate
The case fatality rate of cholera is highly dependent on access to treatment. Untreated severe cholera (cholera gravis) has a CFR of 25 to 50 percent or even higher. With prompt and appropriate oral rehydration therapy (ORT), CFR can be reduced to below 1 percent. With intravenous rehydration for the most severe cases plus antibiotic therapy, CFR can be reduced to well below 0.5 percent in well-resourced settings. The challenge is that outbreaks frequently occur in settings with limited healthcare access, where CFRs may remain high (5-10 percent or more). During the 2010 Haiti outbreak, CFR was initially around 2-3 percent but varied across regions. During the Yemen outbreak, CFR was approximately 0.3 percent in areas with healthcare access but much higher in remote regions. Emergency cholera treatment centers (CTCs) using standardized cholera kit protocols can achieve very low CFRs even in resource-limited settings.

# Diagnosis
Cholera can be diagnosed clinically — the combination of acute watery diarrhea, rice-water stools, and severe dehydration in an endemic or outbreak setting is highly suggestive. Laboratory confirmation is by isolation and identification of V. cholerae O1 or O139 from stool culture on selective media (thiosulfate citrate bile salts sucrose agar, TCBS), with confirmation by agglutination with specific antisera. Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) based on immunochromatography can detect V. cholerae O1 and O139 antigens in stool within 15 minutes and are valuable for field use in outbreak settings. PCR-based methods offer high sensitivity and specificity and can detect antibiotic resistance genes. In outbreak settings, clinical diagnosis is typically sufficient to begin treatment, with laboratory confirmation reserved for confirming the outbreak and characterizing the strain.

# Treatment
The cornerstone of cholera treatment is rapid, aggressive rehydration to replace fluid and electrolyte losses. Oral rehydration salts (ORS) — a specific formulation of glucose, sodium chloride, potassium chloride, and trisodium citrate dissolved in water — exploit the glucose-sodium co-transporter in the gut to drive sodium and water absorption even in the presence of cholera toxin. ORS is effective for approximately 80 percent of cholera patients with mild to moderate dehydration. Severely dehydrated patients require rapid intravenous rehydration with Ringer's lactate or normal saline before switching to oral rehydration. Antibiotic therapy with azithromycin, tetracycline, or doxycycline reduces the duration of diarrhea, the volume of fluids required, and the duration of V. cholerae excretion, but is reserved for moderate to severe cases due to concerns about antibiotic resistance. Zinc supplementation reduces the duration and severity of diarrhea in children under 5 years. Critically, food intake should not be withheld — continued feeding (including breastfeeding for infants) supports gut recovery.

# Vaccines
Two WHO-prequalified oral cholera vaccines (OCVs) are available. Dukoral (WC-rBS) is a killed whole-cell vaccine with recombinant cholera toxin B subunit, requiring two doses (three for children under 6) and providing approximately 85 percent short-term protection declining to 50-60 percent at 3 years. Shanchol and Euvichol-Plus are monovalent killed whole-cell vaccines available without the B subunit, requiring two doses and providing similar efficacy. The global OCV stockpile, managed by the International Coordinating Group (ICG), is used to respond to cholera outbreaks in emergency settings. Demand for OCV has greatly exceeded supply in recent years, leading to temporary shift to single-dose campaigns (providing approximately 6 months protection) to stretch limited supplies during the current global crisis.

# Prevention and WHO Guidelines
WHO's Global Task Force on Cholera Control (GTFCC) coordinates the global Ending Cholera: A Global Roadmap to 2030 strategy, which aims to reduce cholera deaths by 90 percent by 2030. Prevention strategies are built on three pillars. First, early detection and response — robust surveillance systems to rapidly detect and confirm outbreaks, combined with prepositioning of OCV and treatment supplies. Second, multisectoral approach — investment in safe water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) infrastructure as the long-term solution to endemic cholera; promotion of handwashing with soap; safe food handling and preparation; and water treatment at household and community level. Third, oral cholera vaccines — strategic use of OCV in endemic areas and outbreak response to provide bridge protection while longer-term WASH solutions are implemented. Additionally, community engagement and health education are essential to promote preventive behaviors and ensure early care-seeking. In outbreak settings, establishment of cholera treatment centers with standardized case management protocols is critical for reducing mortality.

# Healthcare System Impact
Outbreaks of this disease place enormous strain on healthcare systems, particularly in low-resource settings. Healthcare workers face significant occupational risk during outbreaks and require adequate personal protective equipment, training, and psychosocial support. Outbreaks divert critical healthcare resources from routine services, leading to indirect mortality from untreated non-outbreak conditions. Strengthening health system resilience — including robust surveillance infrastructure, trained rapid response teams, stockpiles of essential supplies, and community trust in health authorities — is fundamental to effective outbreak preparedness and response. International surge capacity support through mechanisms such as the WHO Global Outbreak Alert and Response Network (GOARN) plays a critical role in supporting affected countries during major outbreaks.

# Equity and Social Determinants
The burden of this disease falls disproportionately on the world's most vulnerable populations — those living in poverty, conflict zones, areas with inadequate infrastructure, and communities that have experienced historical marginalization and discrimination. Social determinants including poverty, food insecurity, lack of access to clean water and sanitation, crowded living conditions, limited healthcare access, and educational inequities all amplify disease risk and impair the ability of individuals and communities to protect themselves and access treatment. Meaningful, sustainable progress requires not only biomedical interventions but also systemic action on these social, economic, and political determinants of health. Community engagement — treating affected communities as partners rather than objects of interventions — is essential for building the trust required for effective outbreak response. Stigma associated with infectious disease outbreaks can deter people from seeking care and reporting illness, amplifying transmission and mortality.

# Research and Innovation
Ongoing research continues to advance understanding of this disease and improve tools for prevention, diagnosis, and treatment. Areas of active investigation include development of next-generation vaccines with improved efficacy, durability, and breadth of protection; novel therapeutic agents targeting key steps in the disease process; improved rapid diagnostic tools for use at the point of care in resource-limited settings; genomic epidemiology tools for real-time outbreak tracking and source attribution; mathematical models for predicting outbreak dynamics and optimizing response strategies; implementation science to understand how to most effectively deliver existing interventions in real-world settings; and social science research to understand community perceptions, behaviors, and responses to outbreaks. International research collaboration — exemplified by the extraordinary scientific response to COVID-19 — has demonstrated the power of global scientific solidarity in accelerating the development of life-saving tools. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared with the populations most affected by these diseases is both an ethical imperative and a practical necessity for effective global health security.

# Global Health Security
This disease illustrates the critical importance of global health security — the capacity of countries to prevent, detect, and respond to public health threats of international concern. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005) provide the legal framework for global health security, requiring countries to develop core public health capacities and to report events that may constitute public health emergencies of international concern. However, many low-income countries lack the resources to fully implement the IHR, creating gaps in the global health security architecture that increase the risk of international spread of outbreaks. The WHO's Joint External Evaluation (JEE) tool allows countries to assess their health security capacities and identify areas for improvement. Sustained international financing for health system strengthening in low-income countries — including infectious disease surveillance, laboratory capacity, health workforce, emergency operations centers, and risk communication — is essential for building the global health security architecture needed to protect all countries from outbreak threats.
