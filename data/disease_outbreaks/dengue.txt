# DENGUE FEVER: COMPREHENSIVE OUTBREAK INTELLIGENCE REPORT
Source: World Health Organization (WHO), CDC, Pan American Health Organization (PAHO)

# Overview
Dengue fever is a mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the dengue virus (DENV), a member of the Flaviviridae family and Flavivirus genus. There are four distinct but closely related serotypes of dengue virus: DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, and DENV-4. Infection with one serotype provides lifelong immunity to that serotype but only partial and temporary cross-protection against the other three. Secondary infection with a different serotype dramatically increases the risk of severe dengue, a potentially fatal condition. Dengue is the most rapidly spreading mosquito-borne viral disease in the world and a major public health concern across tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas, Southeast Asia, the Western Pacific, Africa, and the Eastern Mediterranean.

# Outbreak History
Dengue has been recognized as a disease for centuries — the first recognized epidemic of dengue-like illness occurred in 1779-1780 across Asia, Africa, and North America. The modern pandemic began in Southeast Asia after World War II and has progressively worsened over the past 50 years. Global dengue incidence has increased approximately 30-fold over the past five decades, driven by urbanization, population growth, increased international travel, inadequate vector control, and climate change expanding the geographic range of the Aedes aegypti mosquito. Before 1970, only 9 countries had experienced severe dengue epidemics. Today, dengue is endemic in more than 100 countries.

Major historical outbreaks include the 1953-1954 Philippines outbreak, which was among the first documented outbreaks of dengue hemorrhagic fever (severe dengue). The 1975 Southeast Asia epidemic caused tens of thousands of severe dengue cases across multiple countries. Cuba experienced a major outbreak in 1981 involving over 340,000 cases, introducing DENV-2 into a population previously exposed to DENV-1, producing high rates of severe disease — directly validating the immune enhancement hypothesis. Brazil has experienced recurring massive outbreaks — the 2019 outbreak caused over 2.2 million cases, and the 2023-2024 season produced a record-breaking approximately 4.2 million cases in Brazil alone, with thousands of deaths.

The Americas have experienced an extraordinary escalation in dengue burden over the past decade. The WHO declared dengue an alarming global threat in 2019 when over 5.2 million cases were reported worldwide — the highest ever recorded at that time. The 2023-2024 global dengue season has shattered all previous records, with the Americas alone reporting over 10 million cases and more than 5,000 deaths by mid-2024, driven by El Niño-related climate conditions, co-circulation of multiple serotypes, and urbanization. The WHO declared a global dengue emergency in 2024 as cases reached historically unprecedented levels across multiple continents simultaneously.

# Global Statistics
Dengue imposes an enormous and rapidly growing global burden. The WHO estimates that 390 million dengue infections occur annually globally, of which approximately 96 million manifest clinically. Approximately 3.9 billion people in over 130 countries are at risk of dengue infection. Dengue causes approximately 40,000 deaths per year globally, though this figure is likely an underestimate due to misdiagnosis and underreporting. The economic burden of dengue is substantial — a 2016 study estimated the global economic cost of dengue at US$8.9 billion annually, including direct healthcare costs and indirect costs from lost productivity.

Southeast Asia accounts for approximately 70 percent of the global dengue burden. The five countries with the highest dengue burden are Brazil, the Philippines, Vietnam, Indonesia, and India. Dengue is the leading cause of hospitalization among children in many Asian and Latin American countries. Urban and peri-urban areas are particularly affected due to the proliferation of Aedes aegypti breeding sites in man-made containers.

# Transmission
Dengue is transmitted primarily by Aedes aegypti mosquitoes, which are found in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. Aedes albopictus (the tiger mosquito) is a secondary vector with a broader geographic range, including temperate regions of Europe and North America, and is responsible for dengue transmission in some areas. Unlike Anopheles mosquitoes (malaria vectors), Aedes mosquitoes are daytime biters, with peak biting activity in the early morning and late afternoon. They breed in small collections of stagnant water in and around human habitations — flower pots, discarded tires, water storage containers, clogged gutters, and other artificial containers. This strong association with human environments makes dengue fundamentally a disease of urbanization.

The transmission cycle: A female Aedes mosquito becomes infected when it feeds on a viremic (infectious) human host. The virus replicates in the mosquito's midgut and eventually reaches the salivary glands after an extrinsic incubation period of 8 to 12 days. The infected mosquito can then transmit the virus to susceptible humans through subsequent bites for the rest of its life (2-4 weeks). The intrinsic incubation period in humans (from bite to symptom onset) is 4 to 10 days. Dengue is not directly transmitted from person to person and cannot be spread through contact, respiratory secretions, or contaminated water or food. Rare non-vector transmission routes include blood transfusion, organ transplantation, and vertical transmission from mother to infant.

# Symptoms
Dengue presents across a spectrum of severity. Many infections are asymptomatic (estimated 75 percent of all infections). Symptomatic dengue typically presents as one of three syndromes.

Dengue Fever (Classic Dengue): Characterized by sudden onset of high fever (38.5-40°C), severe headache, pain behind the eyes (retro-orbital pain), muscle and joint pain (historically called "breakbone fever" due to the intensity of musculoskeletal pain), nausea, vomiting, swollen glands, and rash. The rash typically appears 3-5 days after fever onset as a maculopapular or petechial rash. Most patients with dengue fever recover within 7-10 days without specific treatment.

Dengue with Warning Signs: Patients with dengue who develop warning signs are at risk of progression to severe dengue and require urgent medical attention. Warning signs include abdominal pain or tenderness, persistent vomiting, clinical fluid accumulation (ascites, pleural effusion), mucosal bleeding, lethargy or restlessness, liver enlargement greater than 2 cm, and rapid decline in platelet count.

Severe Dengue (Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever/Dengue Shock Syndrome): Characterized by severe plasma leakage leading to shock, severe bleeding, and severe organ involvement (liver, CNS, heart, kidneys). Severe dengue is most common during secondary infections with a different serotype, due to antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE), a mechanism by which non-neutralizing antibodies from the first infection facilitate enhanced viral entry and replication during the second infection. Without proper treatment, severe dengue has a fatality rate of 20 percent or higher; with appropriate management, CFR can be reduced to below 1 percent.

# Case Fatality Rate
The overall case fatality rate of dengue across all cases (including many mild and undetected infections) is less than 1 percent. Among clinically diagnosed dengue cases, CFR is approximately 1 to 5 percent. Among cases of severe dengue (dengue hemorrhagic fever/shock syndrome) without appropriate treatment, CFR can reach 20 percent or higher. With expert clinical management including careful fluid therapy, CFR for severe dengue can be reduced to below 1 percent. Pediatric patients, elderly patients, and those with underlying conditions are at higher risk of fatal outcomes. The highest mortality is seen in settings with limited access to quality hospital care.

# Diagnosis
Dengue diagnosis depends on the phase of illness at the time of testing. During the acute febrile phase (days 1-5 of illness), viral nucleic acid detection by RT-PCR and non-structural protein 1 (NS1) antigen detection are most sensitive. NS1 rapid antigen tests are widely used in clinical settings due to their simplicity and speed. After day 5, when viremia declines, antibody detection using IgM/IgG ELISA becomes more sensitive. IgM antibodies appear approximately 5 days after illness onset and peak at 2 weeks. Dengue-specific IgG appears later in primary infection but rapidly in secondary infection. Full blood count typically shows progressive thrombocytopenia (low platelet count) and leukopenia (low white blood cell count), which are useful clinical indicators but not specific to dengue.

# Treatment
There is no specific antiviral treatment for dengue fever. Management is supportive. For uncomplicated dengue, treatment includes rest, adequate fluid intake (oral rehydration), and paracetamol (acetaminophen) for fever and pain relief. Aspirin and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen are contraindicated due to their antiplatelet effects and risk of gastrointestinal bleeding. Careful monitoring for warning signs is essential. For severe dengue, hospital admission is required with careful intravenous fluid management, close monitoring of hematocrit, platelet count, and vital signs, blood transfusion for severe bleeding, and management of organ complications.

# Vaccines
Dengvaxia (CYD-TDV), developed by Sanofi Pasteur, was the world's first licensed dengue vaccine. However, it carries a significant limitation: in dengue-naïve individuals (those never previously infected with dengue), vaccination increases the risk of severe dengue upon subsequent natural infection, as the vaccine primes the immune system similarly to a primary dengue infection. WHO therefore recommends Dengvaxia only for individuals with confirmed prior dengue infection, verified through serological testing. Qdenga (TAK-003), developed by Takeda Pharmaceutical, is a newer tetravalent dengue vaccine approved in several countries including the European Union, Brazil, and Argentina. Phase 3 trials showed 80.2 percent efficacy against dengue disease and 90.4 percent efficacy against hospitalization in children aged 4-16 years. Qdenga can be used regardless of prior dengue serostatus, making it more broadly applicable than Dengvaxia.

# Prevention and WHO Guidelines
WHO dengue prevention strategies center on integrated vector management (IVM), which combines multiple vector control approaches. Source reduction — eliminating mosquito breeding sites by regularly emptying, cleaning, or covering water storage containers — is the most fundamental prevention measure. Environmental management includes proper solid waste disposal and modification of water storage practices. Biological control using Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis (Bti) and copepods can reduce larval populations in certain water bodies. Chemical control through larviciding and space spraying (fogging) is used during outbreaks. The Wolbachia method — releasing mosquitoes infected with the naturally occurring Wolbachia bacteria, which reduces their ability to transmit dengue — has shown promising results in field trials in multiple countries and is being scaled up. Personal protection through repellents, protective clothing, and window/door screens reduces individual risk. WHO's Global Dengue Control Strategy 2021-2030 aims to reduce dengue mortality by 50 percent and morbidity by 25 percent compared to the 2010-2020 decade, through strengthened surveillance, integrated vector management, case management, and vaccine deployment.

# Healthcare System Impact
Outbreaks of this disease place enormous strain on healthcare systems, particularly in low-resource settings. Healthcare workers face significant occupational risk during outbreaks and require adequate personal protective equipment, training, and psychosocial support. Outbreaks divert critical healthcare resources from routine services, leading to indirect mortality from untreated non-outbreak conditions. Strengthening health system resilience — including robust surveillance infrastructure, trained rapid response teams, stockpiles of essential supplies, and community trust in health authorities — is fundamental to effective outbreak preparedness and response. International surge capacity support through mechanisms such as the WHO Global Outbreak Alert and Response Network (GOARN) plays a critical role in supporting affected countries during major outbreaks.

# Equity and Social Determinants
The burden of this disease falls disproportionately on the world's most vulnerable populations — those living in poverty, conflict zones, areas with inadequate infrastructure, and communities that have experienced historical marginalization and discrimination. Social determinants including poverty, food insecurity, lack of access to clean water and sanitation, crowded living conditions, limited healthcare access, and educational inequities all amplify disease risk and impair the ability of individuals and communities to protect themselves and access treatment. Meaningful, sustainable progress requires not only biomedical interventions but also systemic action on these social, economic, and political determinants of health. Community engagement — treating affected communities as partners rather than objects of interventions — is essential for building the trust required for effective outbreak response. Stigma associated with infectious disease outbreaks can deter people from seeking care and reporting illness, amplifying transmission and mortality.

# Research and Innovation
Ongoing research continues to advance understanding of this disease and improve tools for prevention, diagnosis, and treatment. Areas of active investigation include development of next-generation vaccines with improved efficacy, durability, and breadth of protection; novel therapeutic agents targeting key steps in the disease process; improved rapid diagnostic tools for use at the point of care in resource-limited settings; genomic epidemiology tools for real-time outbreak tracking and source attribution; mathematical models for predicting outbreak dynamics and optimizing response strategies; implementation science to understand how to most effectively deliver existing interventions in real-world settings; and social science research to understand community perceptions, behaviors, and responses to outbreaks. International research collaboration — exemplified by the extraordinary scientific response to COVID-19 — has demonstrated the power of global scientific solidarity in accelerating the development of life-saving tools. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared with the populations most affected by these diseases is both an ethical imperative and a practical necessity for effective global health security.

# Global Health Security
This disease illustrates the critical importance of global health security — the capacity of countries to prevent, detect, and respond to public health threats of international concern. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005) provide the legal framework for global health security, requiring countries to develop core public health capacities and to report events that may constitute public health emergencies of international concern. However, many low-income countries lack the resources to fully implement the IHR, creating gaps in the global health security architecture that increase the risk of international spread of outbreaks. The WHO's Joint External Evaluation (JEE) tool allows countries to assess their health security capacities and identify areas for improvement. Sustained international financing for health system strengthening in low-income countries — including infectious disease surveillance, laboratory capacity, health workforce, emergency operations centers, and risk communication — is essential for building the global health security architecture needed to protect all countries from outbreak threats.
