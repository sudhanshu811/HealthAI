# MALARIA: COMPREHENSIVE OUTBREAK INTELLIGENCE REPORT
Source: World Health Organization (WHO), CDC, Roll Back Malaria Partnership

# Overview
Malaria is a life-threatening infectious disease caused by Plasmodium parasites transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. Five species of Plasmodium cause malaria in humans: P. falciparum (most lethal, dominant in sub-Saharan Africa), P. vivax (most geographically widespread), P. ovale, P. malariae, and P. knowlesi. Malaria is one of the oldest and most persistent infectious diseases in human history, mentioned in ancient Chinese and Indian medical texts and referenced in writings from ancient Greece and Rome. Despite being preventable and curable, malaria continues to kill hundreds of thousands of people annually, making it one of the most significant global public health challenges.

# Outbreak History
Malaria has shaped human history profoundly, influencing the outcomes of military campaigns, colonization, and the development of entire civilizations. Malaria was endemic across large parts of Europe and North America until the 20th century. The introduction of DDT-based vector control in the 1950s and 1960s eliminated malaria from Europe, North America, and parts of Asia and Latin America. A global eradication campaign led by WHO from 1955 to 1969 succeeded in eliminating malaria from many temperate regions but failed to achieve eradication in tropical Africa, where the disease remained highly endemic.

The 1970s and 1980s saw a resurgence of malaria in many parts of the world due to the development of insecticide resistance in mosquitoes and chloroquine resistance in P. falciparum parasites. By the 1990s, malaria was responsible for an estimated one million deaths per year, the vast majority in children under five in sub-Saharan Africa. The 2000 Abuja Declaration, signed by 44 African heads of state, galvanized political commitment and international funding for malaria control. The creation of the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria in 2002 mobilized unprecedented resources for malaria control. The President's Malaria Initiative (PMI), launched by the United States in 2005, further scaled up prevention and treatment programs in high-burden countries. These combined efforts led to dramatic reductions in malaria cases and deaths between 2000 and 2015.

Progress stalled between 2015 and 2020, with the global malaria burden plateauing due to insecticide resistance, funding gaps, and biological challenges. The COVID-19 pandemic severely disrupted malaria services in 2020, contributing to a significant increase in malaria deaths that year. In 2021, WHO reported 247 million malaria cases and 619,000 deaths — a significant increase from pre-pandemic levels. Emerging artemisinin resistance in parts of sub-Saharan Africa, first confirmed in 2023, represents a potentially catastrophic threat to global malaria control.

# Global Statistics
According to the WHO World Malaria Report 2023, there were 249 million malaria cases globally in 2022, occurring in 85 malaria-endemic countries and territories. Malaria caused approximately 608,000 deaths in 2022. The WHO African Region bears a disproportionate burden, accounting for 95 percent of all malaria cases and 96 percent of all malaria deaths. Children under five years of age account for approximately 80 percent of malaria deaths in Africa. Five countries — Nigeria, DRC, Uganda, Mozambique, and Tanzania — account for approximately 50 percent of all global malaria cases. Since 2000, an estimated 1.5 billion malaria cases and 7.6 million deaths have been averted through global malaria control efforts.

# Transmission
Malaria is transmitted exclusively through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. There are approximately 400 species of Anopheles mosquitoes, of which approximately 30 are important malaria vectors. The most important vectors in sub-Saharan Africa are Anopheles gambiae and Anopheles funestus, which are highly efficient vectors due to their strong preference for biting humans (anthropophily) and their tendency to bite indoors at night. In Asia, Anopheles dirus and Anopheles minimus are important vectors. In the Americas, Anopheles darlingi is the dominant vector.

Transmission occurs when an infected mosquito takes a blood meal, injecting sporozoites — the infectious stage of the parasite — into the human bloodstream. Malaria cannot be transmitted through casual contact, coughing, or sneezing. Rare non-mosquito transmission routes include blood transfusion, organ transplantation, sharing of contaminated needles, and mother-to-child transmission (congenital malaria). The parasite undergoes complex development in both the human host (liver and blood stages) and the mosquito (gut and salivary gland stages). The periodicity of fever in malaria — every 48 hours for P. falciparum and P. vivax, every 72 hours for P. malariae — corresponds to the synchronized rupture of infected red blood cells.

# Symptoms
The clinical presentation of malaria varies by species, immune status of the host, and severity of infection. The classic malaria paroxysm consists of three stages: a cold stage (sudden onset of shivering and chills lasting 15-60 minutes), a hot stage (high fever up to 40-41°C, headache, nausea, and vomiting lasting 2-6 hours), and a sweating stage (profuse sweating, resolution of fever, and exhaustion). Other common symptoms include body aches, fatigue, and anemia. In non-immune individuals and young children, illness can rapidly progress from uncomplicated to severe malaria. Severe P. falciparum malaria is characterized by cerebral malaria (coma and seizures), severe anemia, acute respiratory distress, hypoglycemia, renal failure, circulatory collapse, and abnormal bleeding. Severe malaria is a medical emergency with high mortality even with treatment.

# Case Fatality Rate
The case fatality rate of malaria varies considerably by species, age, immune status, access to treatment, and disease severity. Uncomplicated P. falciparum malaria treated promptly with effective antimalarials has a very low CFR (less than 0.1 percent). Severe P. falciparum malaria has a CFR of 15 to 20 percent even with optimal hospital management, and up to 30 percent or higher without treatment. P. vivax was historically considered benign, but severe and fatal P. vivax malaria is increasingly recognized, with a CFR of approximately 0.1 to 0.5 percent in treated patients. Overall, the WHO estimates approximately 608,000 deaths from malaria in 2022, the vast majority in young children in sub-Saharan Africa who lack protective immunity.

# Diagnosis
Accurate diagnosis is essential for appropriate treatment. The gold standard for malaria diagnosis is microscopic examination of Giemsa-stained thick and thin blood smears, which allows species identification and parasite quantification. Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) detect malaria antigens (HRP2 for P. falciparum and pan-malarial antigens for other species) and provide results within 15 to 20 minutes without laboratory equipment, making them invaluable in resource-limited settings. Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is the most sensitive method and can detect very low parasitemia and identify species, but requires laboratory infrastructure and is primarily used for research and reference testing.

# Treatment
The WHO recommends artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) as first-line treatment for uncomplicated P. falciparum malaria in all transmission settings. ACTs combine a rapidly acting artemisinin derivative with a longer-acting partner drug. Commonly used ACTs include artemether-lumefantrine (AL, Coartem), artesunate-amodiaquine (ASAQ), artesunate-mefloquine (ASMQ), and dihydroartemisinin-piperaquine (DP). Severe malaria requires urgent parenteral treatment — intravenous artesunate is the WHO-recommended first-line treatment for severe P. falciparum malaria in adults and children, having been shown superior to quinine in reducing mortality. Chloroquine remains effective for P. vivax in most areas (though chloroquine-resistant P. vivax is emerging in some regions), combined with primaquine or tafenoquine to prevent relapse from liver-stage hypnozoites. Artemisinin resistance confirmed in East Africa in 2023 is a critical concern requiring urgent surveillance and response.

# Vaccines
The RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix) vaccine, developed by GlaxoSmithKline in partnership with PATH and the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, is the world's first malaria vaccine recommended by WHO. In October 2021, WHO recommended broad use of RTS,S among children in sub-Saharan Africa and other regions with moderate to high P. falciparum malaria transmission. RTS,S targets the circumsporozoite protein of P. falciparum and has shown efficacy of approximately 30 to 56 percent against clinical malaria in children aged 5-17 months. The R21/Matrix-M vaccine, developed by the University of Oxford and the Serum Institute of India, received WHO prequalification in 2023 and showed higher efficacy (approximately 75 percent) in trials in Mali and Burkina Faso. Both vaccines are being scaled up across sub-Saharan Africa.

# Prevention and WHO Guidelines
WHO prevention strategies for malaria are built on an integrated approach. Insecticide-treated mosquito nets (ITNs) and long-lasting insecticidal nets (LLINs) are among the most cost-effective interventions, reducing transmission by killing or repelling mosquitoes. Indoor residual spraying (IRS) involves application of residual insecticides to household walls and surfaces. Chemoprevention strategies include seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC) with sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine plus amodiaquine for children in the Sahel region, intermittent preventive treatment in pregnancy (IPTp) with sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine, and perennial malaria chemoprevention (PMC) for infants. Personal protection measures include use of repellents, protective clothing, and avoiding outdoor exposure during peak biting hours (dusk to dawn). Larval source management including drainage of standing water and application of larvicides reduces mosquito breeding sites. The WHO Global Malaria Programme coordinates global malaria strategy and supports countries in developing and implementing national malaria control plans targeting elimination and ultimately eradication.

# Healthcare System Impact
Outbreaks of this disease place enormous strain on healthcare systems, particularly in low-resource settings. Healthcare workers face significant occupational risk during outbreaks and require adequate personal protective equipment, training, and psychosocial support. Outbreaks divert critical healthcare resources from routine services, leading to indirect mortality from untreated non-outbreak conditions. Strengthening health system resilience — including robust surveillance infrastructure, trained rapid response teams, stockpiles of essential supplies, and community trust in health authorities — is fundamental to effective outbreak preparedness and response. International surge capacity support through mechanisms such as the WHO Global Outbreak Alert and Response Network (GOARN) plays a critical role in supporting affected countries during major outbreaks.

# Equity and Social Determinants
The burden of this disease falls disproportionately on the world's most vulnerable populations — those living in poverty, conflict zones, areas with inadequate infrastructure, and communities that have experienced historical marginalization and discrimination. Social determinants including poverty, food insecurity, lack of access to clean water and sanitation, crowded living conditions, limited healthcare access, and educational inequities all amplify disease risk and impair the ability of individuals and communities to protect themselves and access treatment. Meaningful, sustainable progress requires not only biomedical interventions but also systemic action on these social, economic, and political determinants of health. Community engagement — treating affected communities as partners rather than objects of interventions — is essential for building the trust required for effective outbreak response. Stigma associated with infectious disease outbreaks can deter people from seeking care and reporting illness, amplifying transmission and mortality.

# Research and Innovation
Ongoing research continues to advance understanding of this disease and improve tools for prevention, diagnosis, and treatment. Areas of active investigation include development of next-generation vaccines with improved efficacy, durability, and breadth of protection; novel therapeutic agents targeting key steps in the disease process; improved rapid diagnostic tools for use at the point of care in resource-limited settings; genomic epidemiology tools for real-time outbreak tracking and source attribution; mathematical models for predicting outbreak dynamics and optimizing response strategies; implementation science to understand how to most effectively deliver existing interventions in real-world settings; and social science research to understand community perceptions, behaviors, and responses to outbreaks. International research collaboration — exemplified by the extraordinary scientific response to COVID-19 — has demonstrated the power of global scientific solidarity in accelerating the development of life-saving tools. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared with the populations most affected by these diseases is both an ethical imperative and a practical necessity for effective global health security.

# Global Health Security
This disease illustrates the critical importance of global health security — the capacity of countries to prevent, detect, and respond to public health threats of international concern. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005) provide the legal framework for global health security, requiring countries to develop core public health capacities and to report events that may constitute public health emergencies of international concern. However, many low-income countries lack the resources to fully implement the IHR, creating gaps in the global health security architecture that increase the risk of international spread of outbreaks. The WHO's Joint External Evaluation (JEE) tool allows countries to assess their health security capacities and identify areas for improvement. Sustained international financing for health system strengthening in low-income countries — including infectious disease surveillance, laboratory capacity, health workforce, emergency operations centers, and risk communication — is essential for building the global health security architecture needed to protect all countries from outbreak threats.
