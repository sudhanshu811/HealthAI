CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE AND HYPERTENSION: A GLOBAL HEALTH PERSPECTIVE
Source: World Health Organization (WHO) and World Heart Federation

INTRODUCTION

Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) represent a group of disorders affecting the heart and blood vessels, encompassing coronary heart disease, cerebrovascular disease (stroke), peripheral arterial disease, rheumatic heart disease, congenital heart disease, deep vein thrombosis, and pulmonary embolism. CVDs are the leading cause of death globally, accounting for an estimated 17.9 million lives per year, which represents 32 percent of all global deaths. Of these deaths, 85 percent are due to heart attack and stroke. Over three-quarters of CVD deaths occur in low- and middle-income countries.

Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is the single most important risk factor for cardiovascular disease. It is often called the "silent killer" because it rarely causes symptoms even as it silently damages the heart, blood vessels, kidneys, and other organs. The WHO estimates that hypertension affects 1.28 billion adults aged 30 to 79 years worldwide, the majority of whom live in low- and middle-income countries. Alarmingly, approximately 46 percent of adults with hypertension are unaware that they have the condition.

UNDERSTANDING BLOOD PRESSURE

Blood pressure is the force exerted by circulating blood against the walls of the body's arteries. It is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) and recorded as two numbers: systolic pressure (the pressure when the heart beats) over diastolic pressure (the pressure when the heart rests between beats). A normal blood pressure reading is less than 120/80 mmHg. Hypertension is defined as having a systolic blood pressure of 130 mmHg or higher, or a diastolic blood pressure of 80 mmHg or higher, measured on two separate occasions.

Blood pressure levels are classified as normal, elevated, or hypertension stage 1 or stage 2 based on these measurements. When blood pressure consistently remains at elevated levels, it places excessive strain on the heart and arteries, leading to structural and functional changes that increase the risk of serious cardiovascular events.

TYPES OF HYPERTENSION

Primary (Essential) Hypertension: Primary or essential hypertension accounts for 90 to 95 percent of all hypertension cases. It has no identifiable single cause and is believed to result from a complex interaction of genetic, environmental, and lifestyle factors. Risk factors for primary hypertension include unhealthy diet (particularly excess sodium intake), physical inactivity, overweight and obesity, excessive alcohol consumption, tobacco use, stress, and family history of hypertension.

Secondary Hypertension: Secondary hypertension accounts for 5 to 10 percent of cases and is caused by an identifiable underlying condition. Common causes include chronic kidney disease, primary aldosteronism (overproduction of the hormone aldosterone), renovascular disease, obstructive sleep apnea, thyroid disorders, and the use of certain medications such as oral contraceptives, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), and some decongestants. Treatment of the underlying cause can often lead to significant improvement or resolution of secondary hypertension.

CAUSES AND RISK FACTORS FOR CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE

The development of cardiovascular disease is influenced by a combination of modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors. Non-modifiable risk factors include age, sex, family history, and ethnicity. The risk of CVD increases with age, and men are generally at higher risk than premenopausal women, though this difference diminishes after menopause.

Modifiable risk factors are lifestyle-related and can be changed to reduce CVD risk. These include hypertension, which is the most significant modifiable risk factor for CVD; tobacco use, which damages the lining of arteries and accelerates atherosclerosis; dyslipidemia, or abnormal blood cholesterol levels, particularly high levels of LDL (low-density lipoprotein) cholesterol; diabetes mellitus; physical inactivity; unhealthy diet, particularly one high in saturated fat, trans fat, sodium, and added sugars; overweight and obesity; and excessive alcohol consumption. Psychosocial factors such as chronic stress, depression, anxiety, and social isolation are also increasingly recognized as important risk factors for cardiovascular disease.

ATHEROSCLEROSIS: THE UNDERLYING PROCESS

The majority of cardiovascular disease events are caused by atherosclerosis, a condition in which fatty deposits called plaques accumulate inside the walls of arteries. The process of atherosclerosis begins early in life and progresses gradually over decades. Initially, damage to the arterial endothelium (inner lining) allows LDL cholesterol to penetrate the arterial wall, where it undergoes oxidation. This triggers an inflammatory response, attracting immune cells called macrophages, which engulf the oxidized LDL and transform into foam cells. Over time, these foam cells accumulate to form fatty streaks, which eventually develop into atherosclerotic plaques.

As plaques grow, they narrow the arterial lumen, reducing blood flow to downstream tissues. Plaques can also rupture, triggering the formation of blood clots (thrombi) that can partially or completely block blood flow, causing a heart attack if this occurs in a coronary artery or a stroke if it occurs in a cerebral artery.

HEART ATTACK (MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION)

A heart attack occurs when blood flow to a part of the heart muscle is blocked long enough to cause permanent damage or death (necrosis) of heart muscle cells. The most common cause is rupture of an atherosclerotic plaque in a coronary artery, leading to formation of a blood clot that obstructs blood flow. Symptoms of a heart attack include chest pain or discomfort (often described as pressure, tightness, squeezing, or heaviness), pain or discomfort in the arm, neck, jaw, back, or stomach, shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, lightheadedness, and a feeling of impending doom. Women are more likely than men to experience atypical symptoms such as fatigue, nausea, and back pain, which can lead to delays in seeking treatment.

Heart attacks are medical emergencies requiring immediate treatment. The goal of treatment is to restore blood flow to the affected part of the heart as quickly as possible. This can be achieved through thrombolytic therapy (clot-dissolving drugs), percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI, commonly called angioplasty), or coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) surgery.

STROKE

A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is disrupted, either by a blockage (ischemic stroke, accounting for about 87 percent of all strokes) or by bleeding into brain tissue (hemorrhagic stroke). Like heart attacks, strokes are medical emergencies. The acronym FAST is used to recognize stroke symptoms: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, and Time to call emergency services. Other symptoms include sudden numbness or weakness of the face, arm, or leg, especially on one side; sudden confusion or trouble understanding; sudden vision problems; sudden dizziness, loss of balance, or coordination; and sudden severe headache with no known cause.

Ischemic strokes can be treated with thrombolytic therapy (tissue plasminogen activator, or tPA) if administered within a narrow time window, typically within 4.5 hours of symptom onset, or with mechanical thrombectomy. Hemorrhagic strokes require different management approaches, including controlling blood pressure and, in some cases, surgical intervention.

HEART FAILURE

Heart failure is a condition in which the heart is unable to pump sufficient blood to meet the body's needs. It can result from various underlying conditions, including coronary artery disease, hypertension, cardiomyopathy, heart valve disease, and arrhythmias. Symptoms of heart failure include shortness of breath (particularly during exertion or when lying flat), fatigue, swelling in the legs, ankles, and feet, rapid or irregular heartbeat, reduced ability to exercise, persistent cough or wheezing, and frequent urination at night. Heart failure is managed with a combination of lifestyle modifications, medications (such as ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, diuretics, and aldosterone antagonists), and in advanced cases, devices such as implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs), cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT), or heart transplantation.

PREVENTION AND CONTROL

Cardiovascular disease and hypertension are largely preventable through lifestyle modifications and, where necessary, medical treatment. The WHO promotes a comprehensive approach to CVD prevention and control based on population-wide strategies and high-risk individual approaches.

At the population level, strategies include implementing tobacco control policies such as smoke-free legislation, taxation of tobacco products, and cessation support; reducing dietary sodium intake through food labeling requirements and reformulation of processed foods; restricting marketing of unhealthy foods and beverages, particularly to children; implementing fiscal policies to promote healthy diets; creating environments that support physical activity; and reducing harmful alcohol use through taxation and availability restrictions.

At the individual level, the most effective strategies for preventing CVD and managing hypertension include quitting smoking and avoiding secondhand smoke; adopting a heart-healthy diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats while limiting sodium, added sugars, trans fats, and saturated fats; engaging in regular moderate-intensity physical activity of at least 150 minutes per week; maintaining a healthy body weight; limiting alcohol consumption; managing stress; monitoring and controlling blood pressure and blood glucose levels; and taking prescribed medications as directed.

For people with hypertension, antihypertensive medications play an important role in reducing the risk of cardiovascular events. Several classes of drugs are available, including angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), calcium channel blockers, diuretics, and beta-blockers. The choice of medication depends on individual patient characteristics, co-existing conditions, and other factors.

GLOBAL BURDEN AND ECONOMIC IMPACT

The global burden of cardiovascular disease is immense and continues to grow. CVDs are responsible for approximately 17.9 million deaths per year, making them the leading cause of death worldwide. The vast majority of CVD deaths occur in low- and middle-income countries, where healthcare systems are often ill-equipped to provide adequate prevention, detection, and management services. The economic cost of CVD is also enormous, encompassing direct healthcare costs, lost productivity, informal caregiving, and the long-term economic impacts of disability and premature death.

In many low- and middle-income countries, CVDs are now the leading cause of both death and disability, imposing a double burden on already strained healthcare systems and economies. Investing in CVD prevention and control is therefore not only a health imperative but also an economic necessity for sustainable development.

CONCLUSION

Cardiovascular diseases, driven largely by hypertension and other modifiable risk factors, represent the most pressing global public health challenge of our time. The good news is that the vast majority of premature CVD deaths are preventable through lifestyle changes, early detection, and appropriate medical management. Achieving meaningful reductions in the global burden of CVD requires sustained commitment from governments, healthcare systems, communities, and individuals to address the underlying risk factors and ensure equitable access to prevention and treatment services.

CARDIAC REHABILITATION AND SECONDARY PREVENTION

Cardiac rehabilitation is a medically supervised program designed to help people recover from heart attacks, heart failure, and other heart conditions, and to reduce the risk of future cardiovascular events. Comprehensive cardiac rehabilitation programs typically include monitored exercise training, education about heart-healthy living, counseling to reduce stress, and support to help patients quit smoking and make other lifestyle changes. Multiple clinical trials and meta-analyses have demonstrated that participation in cardiac rehabilitation reduces cardiovascular mortality, hospitalization rates, and improves quality of life and functional capacity. Despite the strong evidence base, cardiac rehabilitation is significantly underutilized globally, with participation rates particularly low among women, older adults, and people in low- and middle-income countries.

Secondary prevention — the use of evidence-based therapies to reduce the risk of recurrent cardiovascular events in people who have already had a heart attack, stroke, or other cardiovascular event — is an essential component of cardiovascular care. Key secondary prevention medications include antiplatelet agents (such as aspirin and clopidogrel) to prevent blood clots; statins to lower LDL cholesterol and stabilize atherosclerotic plaques; ACE inhibitors or ARBs to reduce cardiac workload and protect the kidneys; and beta-blockers to reduce heart rate, blood pressure, and myocardial oxygen demand. Adherence to secondary prevention medications is often suboptimal and is associated with significantly increased risk of recurrent cardiovascular events.

ARRHYTHMIAS AND ELECTROPHYSIOLOGY

Heart arrhythmias are disorders of the heart's electrical system that cause the heart to beat too fast (tachycardia), too slow (bradycardia), or irregularly. The most common sustained arrhythmia is atrial fibrillation (AF), which affects approximately 37 million people worldwide. AF is characterized by rapid, irregular electrical activity in the atria that leads to an irregular and often rapid heart rate. It significantly increases the risk of stroke, heart failure, and cardiovascular death. AF is managed with rate control (to slow the ventricular rate), rhythm control (to restore and maintain normal sinus rhythm), and anticoagulation (to prevent stroke).

Other clinically important arrhythmias include ventricular tachycardia and ventricular fibrillation, which are life-threatening arrhythmias that can cause sudden cardiac death; supraventricular tachycardias; and bradyarrhythmias requiring pacemaker implantation. Catheter ablation — a minimally invasive procedure in which radiofrequency energy or cryotherapy is used to destroy abnormal electrical pathways in the heart — has become an important treatment for many arrhythmias. Implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs) are used to prevent sudden cardiac death in high-risk patients by delivering an electric shock to restore normal rhythm when a life-threatening arrhythmia is detected.

PERIPHERAL ARTERIAL DISEASE AND STROKE PREVENTION

Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) is a condition in which atherosclerosis leads to narrowing and obstruction of the arteries that supply blood to the legs, arms, and other organs outside the heart and brain. It most commonly affects the arteries of the legs, causing symptoms ranging from intermittent claudication (pain or cramping in the legs with walking that is relieved by rest) to critical limb ischemia (severe pain at rest and non-healing wounds or ulcers). PAD is associated with a significantly increased risk of cardiovascular events including heart attack and stroke. Severe PAD can lead to amputation if blood flow to the limb cannot be restored.

Stroke prevention is a major priority in cardiovascular medicine. Primary prevention of stroke focuses on control of major risk factors including hypertension, atrial fibrillation, diabetes, and high cholesterol, and on lifestyle modification. Secondary prevention after a transient ischemic attack (TIA) or stroke focuses on antiplatelet therapy, anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation, aggressive blood pressure control, and carotid endarterectomy or carotid artery stenting in appropriate patients with significant carotid artery stenosis.

SOCIAL DETERMINANTS AND CARDIOVASCULAR HEALTH

Cardiovascular health is profoundly shaped by social and economic factors. Poverty, low educational attainment, food insecurity, housing instability, and limited access to healthcare are all associated with higher rates of cardiovascular risk factors and worse cardiovascular outcomes. Racial and ethnic disparities in cardiovascular health are well documented in many countries, reflecting the compounding effects of structural racism, discrimination, and social disadvantage on cardiovascular risk factor prevalence and access to quality care.

Addressing cardiovascular health inequities requires action beyond the healthcare system, including policies that address the social determinants of health such as affordable housing, food access, safe neighborhoods for physical activity, and living wages. The concept of "cardiovascular health equity" emphasizes that every person should have an equal opportunity to achieve optimal cardiovascular health regardless of their social, economic, or demographic circumstances.

DIGITAL HEALTH AND CARDIOVASCULAR CARE

Digital health technologies are increasingly being applied to cardiovascular disease prevention, detection, and management. Smartphone applications and wearable devices can monitor heart rate, physical activity, sleep, and other health metrics, enabling individuals to track and improve their cardiovascular health behaviors. Consumer smartwatches and wearables equipped with single-lead electrocardiogram (ECG) capabilities can now detect atrial fibrillation in real time, enabling early diagnosis of this important arrhythmia outside the clinical setting. Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning algorithms are being developed to analyze ECGs, echocardiograms, and other cardiovascular imaging data with performance comparable to or exceeding expert cardiologists. Remote monitoring technologies allow healthcare providers to track patients' vital signs, symptoms, and device data between clinic visits, enabling earlier detection of deterioration and more timely intervention. Telemedicine has expanded access to specialist cardiovascular care for patients in remote or underserved areas. These technologies hold great promise for improving the prevention, detection, and management of cardiovascular disease at both the individual and population level.

HEALTH EQUITY, SOCIAL DETERMINANTS, AND GLOBAL POLICY

The burden of disease is not distributed equally across populations. Individuals living in poverty, those with limited educational opportunities, racial and ethnic minorities, residents of rural and remote areas, refugees and displaced persons, and other marginalized groups consistently experience higher rates of illness, disability, and premature death compared to more advantaged populations. These disparities in health outcomes reflect systemic inequities in the social, economic, and environmental conditions that shape health — conditions often referred to as the social determinants of health.

Social determinants of health include income and economic stability, access to quality education, access to healthcare, characteristics of the built environment (including housing quality, neighborhood safety, and availability of healthy foods), and social context (including social support, experiences of discrimination, and community cohesion). Addressing these upstream determinants requires action across multiple sectors, including education, housing, employment, agriculture, transportation, and urban planning, as well as within the health sector.

Global health governance has evolved significantly over recent decades, with the establishment of new financing mechanisms, normative frameworks, and partnerships aimed at improving health in low- and middle-income countries. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, Gavi the Vaccine Alliance, the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR), the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, and many other bilateral and multilateral actors have mobilized substantial resources and technical assistance for global health. These efforts have contributed to remarkable improvements in health outcomes in many low- and middle-income countries over the past two decades.

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted by United Nations member states in 2015, set a comprehensive global agenda for health and development through 2030. SDG 3 specifically calls for ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all at all ages, with specific targets related to maternal and child mortality, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, mental health, universal health coverage, and health workforce. Progress toward the SDGs has been uneven, and the COVID-19 pandemic has set back progress across many indicators, underscoring the need for renewed commitment and accelerated action.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a cross-cutting threat that affects the prevention and treatment of a wide range of infectious diseases. The overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human health, veterinary medicine, and agriculture has accelerated the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which are increasingly rendering standard treatments ineffective. AMR is estimated to cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year directly and contributes to millions more deaths when infections that would previously have been easily treated become life-threatening. Addressing AMR requires a One Health approach that recognizes the interconnections between human health, animal health, and environmental health, and encompasses stewardship of existing antimicrobials, development of new ones, strengthening of infection prevention and control, and international cooperation on surveillance and policy.

Research and innovation are essential for continued progress in global health. Investment in basic biomedical research, clinical trials, health systems research, and implementation science is needed to generate new knowledge, develop better tools, and identify the most effective and cost-effective ways to deliver existing interventions at scale. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared — including through mechanisms that facilitate access to medicines and vaccines in low- and middle-income countries — is a fundamental obligation of global health justice.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital health tools and telemedicine, creating new opportunities to extend the reach of health services, improve the continuity of care, and engage patients in their own health management. Ensuring that the benefits of digital health are equitably accessible — and do not exacerbate existing health disparities due to differential access to technology and digital literacy — is an important challenge for health policymakers and implementers.

Strengthening primary health care is recognized as the foundation of universal health coverage and an essential strategy for addressing health inequities. Strong primary health care systems provide comprehensive, accessible, people-centered care close to where people live and work, integrating health promotion, disease prevention, diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care. Community health workers — lay members of the community trained to provide a range of basic health services — play a vital role in extending the reach of primary health care to the most underserved populations.

International health regulations and global health security frameworks provide the legal and institutional foundation for collective action on cross-border health threats. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), administered by the WHO, require countries to develop and maintain core public health capacities for detecting, assessing, notifying, and responding to public health events of international concern. Strengthening these capacities, particularly in low-income countries, is a priority for global health security and for ensuring that the world is better prepared for future health crises.
