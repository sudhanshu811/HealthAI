CANCER: PREVENTION, EARLY DETECTION, AND GLOBAL BURDEN
Source: World Health Organization (WHO) International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC)

INTRODUCTION

Cancer is a broad term for a large group of diseases characterized by the abnormal and uncontrolled growth and spread of cells. It can affect almost any part of the body, and the affected cells can invade adjoining parts of the body and spread to other organs — a process called metastasis. Cancer is the second leading cause of death globally, responsible for approximately 10 million deaths in 2020, or nearly one in six deaths worldwide. An estimated 19.3 million new cancer cases were diagnosed worldwide in 2020, and the global cancer burden is expected to reach 28.4 million cases by 2040, driven by demographic changes (population growth and aging) and increasing exposure to cancer risk factors, particularly in low- and middle-income countries.

Cancer affects people in every country, at every age, and across all socioeconomic groups. However, the burden of cancer is not borne equally. Low- and middle-income countries account for approximately 70 percent of all cancer deaths globally, in large part because of late-stage diagnoses (due to lack of early detection programs and limited access to healthcare) and lack of access to timely and effective treatment.

HOW CANCER DEVELOPS

Cancer develops through a multistep process driven by accumulating genetic mutations in normal cells. These mutations disrupt the normal mechanisms that regulate cell growth, division, and death. The key hallmarks of cancer include sustained proliferative signaling, evasion of growth suppressors, resistance to cell death, replicative immortality, induction of angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation to supply the growing tumor), activation of invasion and metastasis, reprogramming of cellular metabolism, and evasion of immune destruction.

Mutations can arise spontaneously during DNA replication, as a result of exposure to carcinogenic agents (such as tobacco smoke, ultraviolet radiation, or certain chemicals), or through infection with oncogenic viruses (such as human papillomavirus, hepatitis B and C viruses, and Epstein-Barr virus). Inherited germline mutations in tumor suppressor genes or oncogenes can also predispose individuals to cancer. Most cancers require the accumulation of multiple mutations over many years before malignant transformation occurs, which is why cancer is predominantly a disease of older adults, though it can occur at any age.

MAJOR TYPES OF CANCER

The most commonly diagnosed cancers worldwide include lung cancer, colorectal cancer, breast cancer, prostate cancer, stomach cancer, liver cancer, cervical cancer, and non-Hodgkin lymphoma. Each of these cancers has distinct risk factors, natural history, and treatment approaches.

Lung Cancer: Lung cancer is the most common cause of cancer death worldwide, responsible for approximately 1.8 million deaths per year. The overwhelmingly dominant risk factor for lung cancer is tobacco smoking, which is responsible for approximately 85 percent of all lung cancer cases. Other risk factors include occupational exposure to carcinogens (such as asbestos, arsenic, and radon), air pollution, and secondhand smoke. Lung cancer is classified into non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC, which includes adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma) and small cell lung cancer (SCLC). Treatment depends on the type and stage and may include surgery, chemotherapy, radiation therapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy.

Breast Cancer: Breast cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer globally, with an estimated 2.3 million new cases in 2020. Although it primarily affects women, men can also develop breast cancer. Risk factors include advancing age, family history of breast cancer, inherited mutations in the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes, prolonged exposure to endogenous estrogen (early menarche, late menopause, nulliparity), postmenopausal hormone therapy, obesity, alcohol consumption, and radiation exposure. Breast cancer is highly heterogeneous, with multiple subtypes having different biological behaviors, prognoses, and treatment responses. Treatment typically involves a combination of surgery (lumpectomy or mastectomy), radiation therapy, chemotherapy, hormone therapy, and targeted therapies such as trastuzumab (Herceptin) for HER2-positive cancers.

Colorectal Cancer: Colorectal cancer (cancer of the colon or rectum) is the third most commonly diagnosed cancer and the second most common cause of cancer death worldwide. Key risk factors include age, family history of colorectal cancer or polyps, personal history of inflammatory bowel disease, unhealthy diet (particularly high consumption of red and processed meat and low intake of fruits, vegetables, and fiber), physical inactivity, overweight and obesity, tobacco smoking, and heavy alcohol use. Colorectal cancer can often be prevented through regular screening with colonoscopy or other tests that detect and allow removal of precancerous polyps before they become malignant.

Cervical Cancer: Cervical cancer is caused almost entirely by persistent infection with human papillomavirus (HPV), particularly high-risk types HPV 16 and HPV 18. It is the fourth most common cancer in women worldwide and a leading cause of cancer death among women in low- and middle-income countries. Unlike most cancers, cervical cancer is almost entirely preventable through vaccination against HPV and screening with cervical cytology (Pap smear) or HPV testing, followed by treatment of precancerous lesions. WHO has launched a global strategy to accelerate the elimination of cervical cancer as a public health problem, with ambitious targets for HPV vaccination coverage, cervical screening coverage, and access to treatment.

RISK FACTORS FOR CANCER

Approximately 30 to 50 percent of cancers are preventable by avoiding risk factors and implementing existing evidence-based prevention strategies. The most important cancer risk factors globally include tobacco use (the single largest preventable cause of cancer, responsible for approximately 22 percent of cancer deaths), alcohol consumption, overweight and obesity, physical inactivity, unhealthy diet, infections with oncogenic viruses and bacteria (including HPV, hepatitis B and C, and Helicobacter pylori), radiation (both ultraviolet radiation from the sun and ionizing radiation), occupational carcinogen exposure, and environmental pollution.

In addition, certain medications (such as hormone therapies), reproductive factors, and medical procedures involving radiation exposure contribute to cancer risk. Genetic predisposition accounts for a minority of cancers, though it can substantially elevate risk in individuals carrying high-penetrance mutations such as BRCA1, BRCA2, and Lynch syndrome genes.

CANCER PREVENTION STRATEGIES

Given that a large proportion of cancer cases are attributable to modifiable risk factors, cancer prevention offers enormous potential for reducing the global cancer burden. Primary prevention strategies aim to reduce exposure to cancer risk factors. The most impactful primary prevention measures include tobacco control (preventing initiation, supporting cessation, and implementing comprehensive tobacco control policies); reduction of alcohol consumption; achieving and maintaining a healthy body weight; regular physical activity; healthy diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains and low in red and processed meat; HPV vaccination; hepatitis B vaccination; reduction of ultraviolet radiation exposure through sun protection behaviors and avoiding artificial sources of UV radiation; and reduction of occupational and environmental carcinogen exposure.

Secondary prevention involves early detection of cancer at a stage when treatment is more likely to be curative. Population-based cancer screening programs for breast cancer (mammography), cervical cancer (Pap smear or HPV test), colorectal cancer (colonoscopy, stool tests), and in some settings lung cancer (low-dose CT in high-risk individuals) have been shown to reduce cancer mortality when implemented with high coverage and quality.

CANCER TREATMENT AND CARE

Cancer treatment depends on the type and stage of cancer, the patient's overall health and preferences, and the availability of treatment options. The main modalities of cancer treatment include surgery, radiation therapy, systemic therapy (chemotherapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy, and hormone therapy), and palliative care. Increasingly, cancer treatment is guided by genomic profiling of the tumor to identify specific molecular targets for therapy.

Immunotherapy has emerged as a transformative modality in cancer treatment over the past decade. Immune checkpoint inhibitors, which unleash the immune system to attack cancer cells, have produced durable responses in a subset of patients with a wide range of cancer types, including melanoma, lung cancer, bladder cancer, and others. Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy has produced remarkable results in certain blood cancers.

GLOBAL DISPARITIES IN CANCER CARE

Significant global disparities exist in access to cancer diagnosis, treatment, and supportive care. In high-income countries, the five-year survival rate for many cancers has improved substantially due to advances in early detection and treatment. In contrast, in many low- and middle-income countries, access to basic cancer diagnosis and treatment remains severely limited, and the majority of cancers are diagnosed at advanced stages when curative treatment is no longer possible. Expanding access to affordable, high-quality cancer care globally is a major priority for the WHO Cancer Initiative.

CONCLUSION

Cancer represents one of the greatest health challenges of the 21st century. The global burden of cancer is enormous and growing, and the disparities in cancer outcomes between high-income and low-income countries are stark and unjust. However, a substantial proportion of cancer cases are preventable through lifestyle modification and vaccination, and many cancers are curable when detected early and treated effectively. Achieving meaningful progress against cancer globally requires a comprehensive approach encompassing primary prevention, early detection, equitable access to treatment, and continued investment in research and innovation.

ADDITIONAL CONTEXT AND EMERGING RESEARCH

The global health landscape continues to evolve as new research, technologies, and interventions reshape our understanding and management of this health domain. Advances in genomics and precision medicine are enabling more personalized approaches to prevention, diagnosis, and treatment, tailoring interventions to the unique biological, social, and environmental characteristics of individuals and populations. Digital health technologies — including artificial intelligence, machine learning, mobile health applications, telemedicine, and electronic health records — are transforming the delivery of care and expanding access to health services in previously underserved settings.

Research continues to illuminate the complex relationships between this health condition and other aspects of health and well-being. The interconnections between physical and mental health, the role of the gut microbiome in disease risk and progression, the influence of environmental exposures and the built environment, and the lasting effects of adverse childhood experiences are all areas of active investigation that are yielding important new insights with implications for prevention and treatment.

The COVID-19 pandemic has had profound and lasting effects on global health systems, disease surveillance, health behaviors, and the mental and physical health of populations worldwide. For many health conditions, the pandemic led to disruptions in care, increases in undiagnosed cases, delays in treatment, and worsening of outcomes that will take years or decades to fully understand and address. At the same time, the unprecedented scientific response to COVID-19 — including the rapid development of vaccines using novel mRNA technology, the acceleration of clinical trials, and the expansion of global research collaborations — has generated new knowledge, tools, and capabilities with broad applications across many areas of health.

Climate change represents an increasingly urgent threat to global health, with implications for the distribution and burden of many infectious and non-communicable diseases. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and more frequent and intense extreme weather events affect the geographic range and seasonality of vector-borne diseases, the safety and availability of food and water, air quality, and the mental health and displacement of vulnerable populations. Integrating climate considerations into health policy, planning, and practice is becoming an essential dimension of public health action.

Universal health coverage (UHC) — the goal of ensuring that all people can access the health services they need without facing financial hardship — is central to achieving equity in health outcomes globally. Achieving UHC requires strengthening primary health care, expanding the health workforce, improving access to essential medicines and technologies, and ensuring sustainable financing for health systems. International cooperation, global health governance, and shared commitment to health as a fundamental human right are essential foundations for making further progress against disease and improving health for all people worldwide.

Health literacy — the ability of individuals to understand and use health information to make informed decisions about their health — plays an important role in shaping health behaviors and outcomes. Low health literacy is associated with poorer management of chronic conditions, reduced uptake of preventive services, worse health outcomes, and higher healthcare costs. Improving health literacy at the individual and community level, and making health information more accessible and understandable to all, are important strategies for empowering people to take control of their health.

Community engagement and participatory approaches are increasingly recognized as essential for the success of public health programs and interventions. Programs that actively involve communities in the design, implementation, and evaluation of health initiatives are more likely to be culturally appropriate, acceptable, and sustainable. Partnering with community organizations, religious institutions, traditional healers, and other trusted community actors can enhance the reach and effectiveness of health programs, particularly in settings where formal healthcare systems have limited reach or are viewed with mistrust.

The health workforce — doctors, nurses, midwives, community health workers, laboratory scientists, and other health professionals — is the backbone of any health system. Globally, there is a critical shortage of health workers, estimated at approximately 18 million by 2030, with the greatest shortfalls in low- and middle-income countries. Investing in health workforce education, training, and retention; ensuring fair compensation and safe working conditions; and developing innovative models of care that maximize the contributions of all types of health workers are essential priorities for strengthening health systems and improving health outcomes globally.

HEALTH EQUITY, SOCIAL DETERMINANTS, AND GLOBAL POLICY

The burden of disease is not distributed equally across populations. Individuals living in poverty, those with limited educational opportunities, racial and ethnic minorities, residents of rural and remote areas, refugees and displaced persons, and other marginalized groups consistently experience higher rates of illness, disability, and premature death compared to more advantaged populations. These disparities in health outcomes reflect systemic inequities in the social, economic, and environmental conditions that shape health — conditions often referred to as the social determinants of health.

Social determinants of health include income and economic stability, access to quality education, access to healthcare, characteristics of the built environment (including housing quality, neighborhood safety, and availability of healthy foods), and social context (including social support, experiences of discrimination, and community cohesion). Addressing these upstream determinants requires action across multiple sectors, including education, housing, employment, agriculture, transportation, and urban planning, as well as within the health sector.

Global health governance has evolved significantly over recent decades, with the establishment of new financing mechanisms, normative frameworks, and partnerships aimed at improving health in low- and middle-income countries. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, Gavi the Vaccine Alliance, the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR), the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, and many other bilateral and multilateral actors have mobilized substantial resources and technical assistance for global health. These efforts have contributed to remarkable improvements in health outcomes in many low- and middle-income countries over the past two decades.

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted by United Nations member states in 2015, set a comprehensive global agenda for health and development through 2030. SDG 3 specifically calls for ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all at all ages, with specific targets related to maternal and child mortality, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, mental health, universal health coverage, and health workforce. Progress toward the SDGs has been uneven, and the COVID-19 pandemic has set back progress across many indicators, underscoring the need for renewed commitment and accelerated action.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a cross-cutting threat that affects the prevention and treatment of a wide range of infectious diseases. The overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human health, veterinary medicine, and agriculture has accelerated the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which are increasingly rendering standard treatments ineffective. AMR is estimated to cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year directly and contributes to millions more deaths when infections that would previously have been easily treated become life-threatening. Addressing AMR requires a One Health approach that recognizes the interconnections between human health, animal health, and environmental health, and encompasses stewardship of existing antimicrobials, development of new ones, strengthening of infection prevention and control, and international cooperation on surveillance and policy.

Research and innovation are essential for continued progress in global health. Investment in basic biomedical research, clinical trials, health systems research, and implementation science is needed to generate new knowledge, develop better tools, and identify the most effective and cost-effective ways to deliver existing interventions at scale. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared — including through mechanisms that facilitate access to medicines and vaccines in low- and middle-income countries — is a fundamental obligation of global health justice.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital health tools and telemedicine, creating new opportunities to extend the reach of health services, improve the continuity of care, and engage patients in their own health management. Ensuring that the benefits of digital health are equitably accessible — and do not exacerbate existing health disparities due to differential access to technology and digital literacy — is an important challenge for health policymakers and implementers.

Strengthening primary health care is recognized as the foundation of universal health coverage and an essential strategy for addressing health inequities. Strong primary health care systems provide comprehensive, accessible, people-centered care close to where people live and work, integrating health promotion, disease prevention, diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care. Community health workers — lay members of the community trained to provide a range of basic health services — play a vital role in extending the reach of primary health care to the most underserved populations.

International health regulations and global health security frameworks provide the legal and institutional foundation for collective action on cross-border health threats. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), administered by the WHO, require countries to develop and maintain core public health capacities for detecting, assessing, notifying, and responding to public health events of international concern. Strengthening these capacities, particularly in low-income countries, is a priority for global health security and for ensuring that the world is better prepared for future health crises.
