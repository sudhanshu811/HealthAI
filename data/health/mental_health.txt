MENTAL HEALTH: GLOBAL OVERVIEW, DISORDERS, AND STRATEGIES
Source: World Health Organization (WHO) and World Federation for Mental Health

INTRODUCTION

Mental health is defined by the World Health Organization as a state of well-being in which an individual realizes his or her own abilities, can cope with the normal stresses of life, can work productively and fruitfully, and is able to make a contribution to his or her community. Mental health is an integral component of overall health and well-being, and there is no health without mental health. Good mental health is fundamental to the social and economic functioning of individuals, families, and communities.

Mental disorders, also called mental health conditions, comprise a broad range of problems with different symptoms, causes, and impacts on daily life. They are generally characterized by some combination of abnormal thoughts, perceptions, emotions, behavior, and relationships with others. Mental disorders are among the most common health conditions globally. The WHO estimates that one in every eight people in the world — approximately 970 million people — are living with a mental disorder. This number increased significantly during the COVID-19 pandemic, with estimates suggesting that anxiety and depression rose by 25 percent in the first year of the pandemic alone.

Despite the enormous global burden of mental health conditions, most people living with mental disorders receive no treatment. Gaps in mental health care are particularly pronounced in low- and middle-income countries, where the vast majority of the world's people with mental disorders live and where fewer than 2 percent of health budgets are typically allocated to mental health. Stigma, discrimination, inadequate healthcare infrastructure, lack of trained mental health professionals, and high out-of-pocket costs are among the most significant barriers to mental health care globally.

COMMON MENTAL HEALTH DISORDERS

Depression: Depression is one of the most common mental health disorders globally, affecting approximately 280 million people worldwide. It is characterized by persistent sadness, loss of interest or pleasure in activities that were previously enjoyed, feelings of guilt or low self-worth, disturbed sleep or appetite, feelings of tiredness, and poor concentration. Depression can lead to a variety of emotional and physical problems and can decrease a person's ability to function at work and at home. In its most severe form, depression can lead to suicidal thoughts and behavior. Depression is not simply feeling sad; it is a serious medical condition that requires assessment and treatment.

Depression occurs in people of all ages, from all walks of life, and in all countries. It is approximately twice as common in women as in men, though men are often less likely to seek help. Depression can have a single episode but more commonly has multiple recurrences over a person's lifetime. Depression can be triggered or worsened by a variety of factors, including life events (such as bereavement, job loss, or relationship breakdown), chronic health conditions, genetic factors, and social determinants such as poverty, social isolation, and discrimination. Effective treatments include psychotherapy (particularly cognitive-behavioral therapy), antidepressant medications, and a combination of both.

Anxiety Disorders: Anxiety disorders are the most common group of mental disorders, affecting approximately 301 million people worldwide. Anxiety is a normal and often healthy emotion; an anxiety disorder develops when a person regularly feels disproportionate levels of anxiety that interfere with daily functioning. Anxiety disorders include generalized anxiety disorder, characterized by excessive, hard-to-control worry about many different things; panic disorder, characterized by recurrent unexpected panic attacks; social anxiety disorder, characterized by intense fear of social or performance situations; specific phobias, characterized by excessive fear of specific objects or situations; and obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), which were previously classified as anxiety disorders.

Symptoms of anxiety disorders vary depending on the type but commonly include feelings of nervousness, restlessness, or tension; a sense of impending danger or doom; increased heart rate; rapid breathing (hyperventilation); sweating; trembling; feeling weak or tired; difficulty concentrating; sleep problems; and gastrointestinal issues. Anxiety disorders are highly treatable, and most people who receive appropriate treatment experience significant improvement. Effective treatments include psychotherapy, particularly cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), medication (such as antidepressants and anti-anxiety drugs), and self-help strategies.

Bipolar Disorder: Bipolar disorder affects approximately 40 million people worldwide and is characterized by alternating periods of elevated or irritable mood (mania or hypomania) and episodes of depression. During manic or hypomanic episodes, a person may feel elated, energetic, and unusually talkative, require less sleep, engage in risky behaviors, and have racing thoughts. During depressive episodes, the person experiences the symptoms of depression described above. Bipolar disorder can significantly impair a person's ability to function at work, in relationships, and in daily life. The condition is managed with mood-stabilizing medications such as lithium, anticonvulsants, and antipsychotics, often in combination with psychotherapy and lifestyle management strategies.

Schizophrenia: Schizophrenia is a severe mental disorder affecting approximately 24 million people worldwide, or about 1 in 300 people. It is characterized by distortions in thinking, perception, emotions, language, sense of self, and behavior. Common symptoms include hallucinations (hearing, seeing, or feeling things that are not there), delusions (false fixed beliefs), disorganized thinking and speech, grossly disorganized or abnormal motor behavior, and negative symptoms such as flattened affect, reduced emotional expression, and social withdrawal. Schizophrenia typically begins in late adolescence or early adulthood and is more common in men than women. It is a lifelong condition that can be managed but not cured. Treatment typically involves antipsychotic medications, psychosocial support, and rehabilitation programs. People with schizophrenia are at significantly higher risk of premature death due to both suicide and physical health conditions.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): Post-traumatic stress disorder can develop in some people who have experienced or witnessed traumatic events such as natural disasters, serious accidents, terrorist acts, war or combat, rape, or other violent personal assaults. PTSD is characterized by vivid, distressing memories or flashbacks of the traumatic event; nightmares; severe emotional distress or physical reactions to reminders of the trauma; avoidance of thoughts, feelings, or reminders of the trauma; negative changes in thinking and mood; and changes in physical and emotional reactions (hyperarousal). PTSD affects approximately 3.9 percent of the global population, with higher rates among individuals exposed to conflict and violence. Evidence-based treatments for PTSD include trauma-focused cognitive-behavioral therapy, EMDR (eye movement desensitization and reprocessing), and pharmacological treatment.

Eating Disorders: Eating disorders such as anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and binge-eating disorder affect approximately 14 percent of adolescents worldwide. They are characterized by unhealthy eating behaviors and distorted attitudes toward food, weight, and body image. Eating disorders have serious health consequences and have the highest mortality rate of any mental health condition. They require specialized treatment that typically includes nutritional rehabilitation, psychotherapy, and medical monitoring.

MENTAL HEALTH AND SUICIDE

Suicide is a serious global public health problem. An estimated 703,000 people die by suicide each year globally, with many more attempting suicide. Suicide occurs throughout the lifespan and represents the fourth leading cause of death among 15 to 29 year-olds globally. Mental disorders, particularly depression and alcohol use disorders, are the most important risk factors for suicide in high-income countries. Suicide is a complex issue influenced by social, psychological, cultural, and other factors. Prevention strategies include restricting access to means of suicide, media reporting guidelines, social and emotional learning programs, identification and management of mental health conditions, and crisis support services.

DETERMINANTS OF MENTAL HEALTH

Mental health is shaped by a complex interplay of individual, social, and structural factors. Individual-level factors include genetics, personality, cognitive styles, and early-life experiences. Social and environmental factors include socioeconomic status, employment, education, housing, social support, exposure to violence and discrimination, and community cohesion. Structural factors include access to healthcare, mental health policy and legislation, and societal attitudes toward mental health. Adverse childhood experiences, including abuse, neglect, and household dysfunction, have particularly profound and lasting effects on mental health throughout the lifespan.

GLOBAL MENTAL HEALTH POLICY AND ACTION

The WHO has led efforts to strengthen global mental health policy and action through several key initiatives. The Comprehensive Mental Health Action Plan 2013–2030 sets global targets for improving mental health governance, providing community-based mental health and social care services, implementing strategies for promotion and prevention, and strengthening information systems, evidence, and research. The WHO's Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP) aims to scale up care for mental, neurological, and substance use disorders, particularly in low- and middle-income countries, by providing evidence-based guidelines and training tools for non-specialist healthcare providers.

BARRIERS TO MENTAL HEALTH CARE

Despite the availability of effective treatments, less than half of people with mental disorders in high-income countries, and fewer than one in ten in low-income countries, receive adequate care. Multiple factors contribute to this treatment gap. Stigma and discrimination remain major barriers, causing many people to feel shame about their mental health difficulties and preventing them from seeking help. Other barriers include a shortage of mental health professionals, particularly in low- and middle-income countries; inadequate integration of mental health into primary health care; insufficient mental health funding; poor quality of care where services are available; and lack of mental health literacy among the general public and among healthcare providers in non-mental health settings.

PROMOTING MENTAL HEALTH AND WELL-BEING

Promoting mental health and preventing mental disorders requires action at individual, family, community, and societal levels. At the individual level, strategies that support mental health include maintaining a healthy lifestyle (regular physical activity, balanced diet, adequate sleep, and avoiding substance use), building resilience and coping skills, fostering positive relationships and social connections, engaging in meaningful activities, and seeking help early when experiencing mental health difficulties.

At the community and societal level, promoting mental health requires addressing the social determinants of mental health, including poverty, inequality, discrimination, and lack of access to education and employment. School-based mental health programs, workplace mental health initiatives, community awareness campaigns, and policies that protect human rights and reduce discrimination all play important roles in promoting mental health at the population level.

CONCLUSION

Mental health is a fundamental component of human well-being and must be treated with the same urgency as physical health. The global burden of mental disorders is enormous, and the treatment gap remains unconscionably wide. Addressing the global mental health crisis requires sustained investment in mental health services, integration of mental health into primary care, action on the social determinants of mental health, reduction of stigma, and amplification of the voices of people with lived experience of mental health conditions. Every person has the right to mental health, and achieving this goal requires commitment from governments, healthcare systems, communities, and individuals worldwide.

ADDITIONAL CONTEXT AND EMERGING RESEARCH

The global health landscape continues to evolve as new research, technologies, and interventions reshape our understanding and management of this health domain. Advances in genomics and precision medicine are enabling more personalized approaches to prevention, diagnosis, and treatment, tailoring interventions to the unique biological, social, and environmental characteristics of individuals and populations. Digital health technologies — including artificial intelligence, machine learning, mobile health applications, telemedicine, and electronic health records — are transforming the delivery of care and expanding access to health services in previously underserved settings.

Research continues to illuminate the complex relationships between this health condition and other aspects of health and well-being. The interconnections between physical and mental health, the role of the gut microbiome in disease risk and progression, the influence of environmental exposures and the built environment, and the lasting effects of adverse childhood experiences are all areas of active investigation that are yielding important new insights with implications for prevention and treatment.

The COVID-19 pandemic has had profound and lasting effects on global health systems, disease surveillance, health behaviors, and the mental and physical health of populations worldwide. For many health conditions, the pandemic led to disruptions in care, increases in undiagnosed cases, delays in treatment, and worsening of outcomes that will take years or decades to fully understand and address. At the same time, the unprecedented scientific response to COVID-19 — including the rapid development of vaccines using novel mRNA technology, the acceleration of clinical trials, and the expansion of global research collaborations — has generated new knowledge, tools, and capabilities with broad applications across many areas of health.

Climate change represents an increasingly urgent threat to global health, with implications for the distribution and burden of many infectious and non-communicable diseases. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and more frequent and intense extreme weather events affect the geographic range and seasonality of vector-borne diseases, the safety and availability of food and water, air quality, and the mental health and displacement of vulnerable populations. Integrating climate considerations into health policy, planning, and practice is becoming an essential dimension of public health action.

Universal health coverage (UHC) — the goal of ensuring that all people can access the health services they need without facing financial hardship — is central to achieving equity in health outcomes globally. Achieving UHC requires strengthening primary health care, expanding the health workforce, improving access to essential medicines and technologies, and ensuring sustainable financing for health systems. International cooperation, global health governance, and shared commitment to health as a fundamental human right are essential foundations for making further progress against disease and improving health for all people worldwide.

Health literacy — the ability of individuals to understand and use health information to make informed decisions about their health — plays an important role in shaping health behaviors and outcomes. Low health literacy is associated with poorer management of chronic conditions, reduced uptake of preventive services, worse health outcomes, and higher healthcare costs. Improving health literacy at the individual and community level, and making health information more accessible and understandable to all, are important strategies for empowering people to take control of their health.

Community engagement and participatory approaches are increasingly recognized as essential for the success of public health programs and interventions. Programs that actively involve communities in the design, implementation, and evaluation of health initiatives are more likely to be culturally appropriate, acceptable, and sustainable. Partnering with community organizations, religious institutions, traditional healers, and other trusted community actors can enhance the reach and effectiveness of health programs, particularly in settings where formal healthcare systems have limited reach or are viewed with mistrust.

The health workforce — doctors, nurses, midwives, community health workers, laboratory scientists, and other health professionals — is the backbone of any health system. Globally, there is a critical shortage of health workers, estimated at approximately 18 million by 2030, with the greatest shortfalls in low- and middle-income countries. Investing in health workforce education, training, and retention; ensuring fair compensation and safe working conditions; and developing innovative models of care that maximize the contributions of all types of health workers are essential priorities for strengthening health systems and improving health outcomes globally.

HEALTH EQUITY, SOCIAL DETERMINANTS, AND GLOBAL POLICY

The burden of disease is not distributed equally across populations. Individuals living in poverty, those with limited educational opportunities, racial and ethnic minorities, residents of rural and remote areas, refugees and displaced persons, and other marginalized groups consistently experience higher rates of illness, disability, and premature death compared to more advantaged populations. These disparities in health outcomes reflect systemic inequities in the social, economic, and environmental conditions that shape health — conditions often referred to as the social determinants of health.

Social determinants of health include income and economic stability, access to quality education, access to healthcare, characteristics of the built environment (including housing quality, neighborhood safety, and availability of healthy foods), and social context (including social support, experiences of discrimination, and community cohesion). Addressing these upstream determinants requires action across multiple sectors, including education, housing, employment, agriculture, transportation, and urban planning, as well as within the health sector.

Global health governance has evolved significantly over recent decades, with the establishment of new financing mechanisms, normative frameworks, and partnerships aimed at improving health in low- and middle-income countries. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, Gavi the Vaccine Alliance, the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR), the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, and many other bilateral and multilateral actors have mobilized substantial resources and technical assistance for global health. These efforts have contributed to remarkable improvements in health outcomes in many low- and middle-income countries over the past two decades.

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted by United Nations member states in 2015, set a comprehensive global agenda for health and development through 2030. SDG 3 specifically calls for ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all at all ages, with specific targets related to maternal and child mortality, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, mental health, universal health coverage, and health workforce. Progress toward the SDGs has been uneven, and the COVID-19 pandemic has set back progress across many indicators, underscoring the need for renewed commitment and accelerated action.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a cross-cutting threat that affects the prevention and treatment of a wide range of infectious diseases. The overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human health, veterinary medicine, and agriculture has accelerated the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which are increasingly rendering standard treatments ineffective. AMR is estimated to cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year directly and contributes to millions more deaths when infections that would previously have been easily treated become life-threatening. Addressing AMR requires a One Health approach that recognizes the interconnections between human health, animal health, and environmental health, and encompasses stewardship of existing antimicrobials, development of new ones, strengthening of infection prevention and control, and international cooperation on surveillance and policy.

Research and innovation are essential for continued progress in global health. Investment in basic biomedical research, clinical trials, health systems research, and implementation science is needed to generate new knowledge, develop better tools, and identify the most effective and cost-effective ways to deliver existing interventions at scale. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared — including through mechanisms that facilitate access to medicines and vaccines in low- and middle-income countries — is a fundamental obligation of global health justice.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital health tools and telemedicine, creating new opportunities to extend the reach of health services, improve the continuity of care, and engage patients in their own health management. Ensuring that the benefits of digital health are equitably accessible — and do not exacerbate existing health disparities due to differential access to technology and digital literacy — is an important challenge for health policymakers and implementers.

Strengthening primary health care is recognized as the foundation of universal health coverage and an essential strategy for addressing health inequities. Strong primary health care systems provide comprehensive, accessible, people-centered care close to where people live and work, integrating health promotion, disease prevention, diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care. Community health workers — lay members of the community trained to provide a range of basic health services — play a vital role in extending the reach of primary health care to the most underserved populations.

International health regulations and global health security frameworks provide the legal and institutional foundation for collective action on cross-border health threats. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), administered by the WHO, require countries to develop and maintain core public health capacities for detecting, assessing, notifying, and responding to public health events of international concern. Strengthening these capacities, particularly in low-income countries, is a priority for global health security and for ensuring that the world is better prepared for future health crises.
