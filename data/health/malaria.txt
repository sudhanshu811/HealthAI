MALARIA: CAUSES, PREVENTION, TREATMENT, AND GLOBAL IMPACT
Source: World Health Organization (WHO) Global Malaria Programme

INTRODUCTION

Malaria is a life-threatening disease caused by parasites of the Plasmodium genus that are transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. It is one of the oldest known human diseases and remains one of the most significant infectious disease threats to global public health today. Malaria is preventable and curable, yet it continues to kill hundreds of thousands of people every year, the vast majority of whom are young children in sub-Saharan Africa.

According to the World Health Organization's World Malaria Report, there were an estimated 249 million cases of malaria globally in 2022, occurring in 85 countries. The global death toll from malaria stands at approximately 608,000 deaths per year. The African Region bears a disproportionately large share of the global malaria burden, accounting for approximately 95 percent of all malaria cases and 96 percent of all malaria deaths. Children under five years of age are the most vulnerable group, accounting for about 80 percent of all malaria deaths in the African region.

THE MALARIA PARASITE AND ITS LIFE CYCLE

Malaria is caused by protozoan parasites of the genus Plasmodium. Five species of Plasmodium cause malaria in humans: Plasmodium falciparum, Plasmodium vivax, Plasmodium ovale, Plasmodium malariae, and Plasmodium knowlesi. Of these, Plasmodium falciparum is the most dangerous, responsible for the majority of severe malaria cases and deaths, and is most prevalent in sub-Saharan Africa. Plasmodium vivax is the most geographically widespread species, found in Asia, Latin America, and some parts of Africa, and is notable for its ability to form dormant liver stages (hypnozoites) that can reactivate and cause relapses weeks to months after the initial infection.

The malaria parasite has a complex life cycle involving both human and mosquito hosts. When an infected female Anopheles mosquito bites a human, it injects sporozoites — the infectious stage of the parasite — into the bloodstream. The sporozoites travel to the liver, where they invade liver cells and undergo asexual reproduction to form thousands of merozoites. After 7 to 30 days, depending on the species, the infected liver cells burst and release merozoites into the bloodstream. These merozoites invade red blood cells, where they continue to multiply asexually. The rupture of infected red blood cells releases more merozoites, causing the periodic fevers and chills characteristic of malaria. Some parasites develop into sexual forms called gametocytes, which are taken up by another mosquito during a blood meal and undergo sexual reproduction in the mosquito's gut, eventually migrating to the salivary glands where they can be transmitted to a new human host.

SYMPTOMS AND CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS

The symptoms of malaria typically appear 10 to 15 days after the infective mosquito bite, though this period can range from 7 days to several months depending on the Plasmodium species. The classic presentation of malaria includes fever, headache, chills, and sweating. Other common symptoms include fatigue, nausea, vomiting, muscle pain, and anemia. In uncomplicated malaria, symptoms typically follow a cyclic pattern corresponding to the rupture of infected red blood cells — every 48 hours for P. falciparum, P. vivax, and P. ovale, and every 72 hours for P. malariae.

Severe malaria, caused primarily by P. falciparum, is a medical emergency and can be rapidly fatal if not treated promptly and correctly. Severe malaria is defined by one or more of the following clinical features: impaired consciousness or coma (cerebral malaria), severe anemia, acute respiratory distress syndrome, hypoglycemia, renal failure, circulatory collapse or shock, spontaneous bleeding, hyperparasitemia (high levels of parasites in the blood), jaundice, and recurrent convulsions. Cerebral malaria, in which parasites cause dysfunction and inflammation in the brain, is particularly serious and can cause permanent neurological damage or death even with treatment.

DIAGNOSIS

Accurate diagnosis is essential for effective malaria management and for rational use of antimalarial drugs. The two main methods of malaria diagnosis are microscopic examination of blood smears and rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs).

Microscopic examination of thick and thin blood smears stained with Giemsa dye is the gold standard for malaria diagnosis. It allows identification of the parasite species and quantification of parasite density, which is important for assessing disease severity and treatment response. However, it requires trained laboratory personnel and reliable equipment. Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) detect malaria parasite antigens in a patient's blood sample using a simple lateral flow immunoassay similar to a home pregnancy test. They can be performed quickly without laboratory equipment and are particularly useful in remote settings where microscopy is not available. WHO recommends that all suspected malaria cases be confirmed by either microscopy or RDT before treatment is initiated.

TREATMENT

Malaria is a curable disease if diagnosed and treated promptly and correctly. The choice of antimalarial treatment depends on the infecting Plasmodium species, the severity of disease, the geographic area where infection was acquired (due to varying patterns of drug resistance), patient characteristics (age, pregnancy status, and any co-existing conditions), and the availability of drugs.

Artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) are the recommended treatment for uncomplicated P. falciparum malaria in most parts of the world. ACTs combine an artemisinin derivative (such as artemether, artesunate, or dihydroartemisinin) with a partner drug that has a longer half-life. The artemisinin component acts rapidly to reduce parasite density, while the partner drug eliminates remaining parasites and helps prevent resistance. Common ACTs include artemether-lumefantrine, artesunate-amodiaquine, artesunate-mefloquine, and dihydroartemisinin-piperaquine.

Severe malaria requires urgent parenteral treatment. Intravenous artesunate is the recommended first-line treatment for severe P. falciparum malaria in adults and children worldwide. It has been shown to be superior to intravenous quinine in terms of survival and tolerability. Patients with severe malaria should be treated in a hospital or facility capable of managing life-threatening complications.

For P. vivax and P. ovale malaria, treatment with chloroquine (in areas without chloroquine resistance) or an ACT (in areas with chloroquine resistance) is required to clear parasites from the blood. Additionally, a course of primaquine is necessary to eliminate hypnozoites from the liver and prevent relapses. Primaquine can cause hemolytic anemia in people with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency, so testing for G6PD deficiency before prescribing primaquine is recommended where feasible.

Drug resistance is a major threat to global malaria control efforts. Resistance to older antimalarials such as chloroquine and sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine is now widespread in many malaria-endemic regions. Emerging resistance to artemisinin and its partner drugs has been detected in Southeast Asia and, more recently, in parts of sub-Saharan Africa, raising serious concerns about the future of malaria treatment. Surveillance of drug resistance patterns and development of new antimalarial drugs are therefore critical priorities.

PREVENTION

Multiple tools and strategies are available for malaria prevention, and effective malaria control requires the integrated use of several approaches.

Insecticide-Treated Mosquito Nets (ITNs) and Long-Lasting Insecticidal Nets (LLINs): ITNs and LLINs are among the most cost-effective and widely used tools for malaria prevention. They provide a physical barrier against mosquito bites and the insecticide kills or repels mosquitoes that come into contact with the net. Regular and consistent use of LLINs during sleeping hours, particularly by children under five and pregnant women, can significantly reduce malaria transmission.

Indoor Residual Spraying (IRS): Indoor residual spraying involves the application of residual insecticides to the inner walls and ceilings of houses and other dwellings. It works by killing mosquitoes that rest on sprayed surfaces after taking a blood meal. IRS is an important malaria prevention tool in areas with high malaria transmission.

Chemoprevention: Preventive antimalarial treatment is recommended for specific high-risk groups. Seasonal malaria chemoprevention (SMC) involves the administration of full treatment courses of antimalarials to children in the Sahel sub-region of Africa during periods of high malaria transmission. Intermittent preventive treatment in pregnancy (IPTp) involves the administration of preventive doses of sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine to pregnant women in areas of moderate to high malaria transmission. Intermittent preventive treatment in infants (IPTi) involves the administration of preventive doses of antimalarials to infants at specific age milestones through routine childhood vaccination programs.

Malaria Vaccines: The development of effective malaria vaccines represents a major milestone in the fight against malaria. The RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix) vaccine, the world's first malaria vaccine recommended by WHO, has been shown in large-scale trials to substantially reduce malaria illness and severe malaria in young African children. WHO recommended the broad use of this vaccine among children living in sub-Saharan Africa and other regions with moderate to high P. falciparum malaria transmission in 2021. A second malaria vaccine, R21/Matrix-M, developed by the University of Oxford and the Serum Institute of India, has also shown high efficacy and received WHO prequalification. Vaccination programs are being scaled up across Africa in coordination with other malaria prevention measures.

GLOBAL PROGRESS AND CHALLENGES

Remarkable progress has been made in the fight against malaria over the past two decades. Between 2000 and 2022, the global malaria incidence rate (cases per 1,000 population at risk) fell by 28 percent, and the global malaria mortality rate fell by 50 percent. Millions of lives have been saved through the scale-up of preventive measures such as ITNs and IRS and the widespread deployment of ACTs.

However, progress has stalled in recent years. The COVID-19 pandemic severely disrupted malaria services, leading to increases in malaria cases and deaths in 2020. Emerging drug resistance, insecticide resistance in mosquito populations, climate change leading to expansion of suitable habitats for malaria-transmitting mosquitoes, and inadequate funding for malaria control programs all pose serious challenges to continued progress.

CONCLUSION

Malaria remains one of the most devastating infectious diseases in the world, killing hundreds of thousands of people each year, mostly young children in sub-Saharan Africa. However, the disease is preventable and curable, and remarkable progress has been achieved over the past two decades through the widespread use of effective prevention and treatment tools. Sustaining and accelerating this progress requires sustained funding, innovative new tools, robust health systems, and strong political commitment from governments, international organizations, and the global community.

ADDITIONAL CONTEXT AND EMERGING RESEARCH

The global health landscape continues to evolve as new research, technologies, and interventions reshape our understanding and management of this health domain. Advances in genomics and precision medicine are enabling more personalized approaches to prevention, diagnosis, and treatment, tailoring interventions to the unique biological, social, and environmental characteristics of individuals and populations. Digital health technologies — including artificial intelligence, machine learning, mobile health applications, telemedicine, and electronic health records — are transforming the delivery of care and expanding access to health services in previously underserved settings.

Research continues to illuminate the complex relationships between this health condition and other aspects of health and well-being. The interconnections between physical and mental health, the role of the gut microbiome in disease risk and progression, the influence of environmental exposures and the built environment, and the lasting effects of adverse childhood experiences are all areas of active investigation that are yielding important new insights with implications for prevention and treatment.

The COVID-19 pandemic has had profound and lasting effects on global health systems, disease surveillance, health behaviors, and the mental and physical health of populations worldwide. For many health conditions, the pandemic led to disruptions in care, increases in undiagnosed cases, delays in treatment, and worsening of outcomes that will take years or decades to fully understand and address. At the same time, the unprecedented scientific response to COVID-19 — including the rapid development of vaccines using novel mRNA technology, the acceleration of clinical trials, and the expansion of global research collaborations — has generated new knowledge, tools, and capabilities with broad applications across many areas of health.

Climate change represents an increasingly urgent threat to global health, with implications for the distribution and burden of many infectious and non-communicable diseases. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and more frequent and intense extreme weather events affect the geographic range and seasonality of vector-borne diseases, the safety and availability of food and water, air quality, and the mental health and displacement of vulnerable populations. Integrating climate considerations into health policy, planning, and practice is becoming an essential dimension of public health action.

Universal health coverage (UHC) — the goal of ensuring that all people can access the health services they need without facing financial hardship — is central to achieving equity in health outcomes globally. Achieving UHC requires strengthening primary health care, expanding the health workforce, improving access to essential medicines and technologies, and ensuring sustainable financing for health systems. International cooperation, global health governance, and shared commitment to health as a fundamental human right are essential foundations for making further progress against disease and improving health for all people worldwide.

Health literacy — the ability of individuals to understand and use health information to make informed decisions about their health — plays an important role in shaping health behaviors and outcomes. Low health literacy is associated with poorer management of chronic conditions, reduced uptake of preventive services, worse health outcomes, and higher healthcare costs. Improving health literacy at the individual and community level, and making health information more accessible and understandable to all, are important strategies for empowering people to take control of their health.

Community engagement and participatory approaches are increasingly recognized as essential for the success of public health programs and interventions. Programs that actively involve communities in the design, implementation, and evaluation of health initiatives are more likely to be culturally appropriate, acceptable, and sustainable. Partnering with community organizations, religious institutions, traditional healers, and other trusted community actors can enhance the reach and effectiveness of health programs, particularly in settings where formal healthcare systems have limited reach or are viewed with mistrust.

The health workforce — doctors, nurses, midwives, community health workers, laboratory scientists, and other health professionals — is the backbone of any health system. Globally, there is a critical shortage of health workers, estimated at approximately 18 million by 2030, with the greatest shortfalls in low- and middle-income countries. Investing in health workforce education, training, and retention; ensuring fair compensation and safe working conditions; and developing innovative models of care that maximize the contributions of all types of health workers are essential priorities for strengthening health systems and improving health outcomes globally.

HEALTH EQUITY, SOCIAL DETERMINANTS, AND GLOBAL POLICY

The burden of disease is not distributed equally across populations. Individuals living in poverty, those with limited educational opportunities, racial and ethnic minorities, residents of rural and remote areas, refugees and displaced persons, and other marginalized groups consistently experience higher rates of illness, disability, and premature death compared to more advantaged populations. These disparities in health outcomes reflect systemic inequities in the social, economic, and environmental conditions that shape health — conditions often referred to as the social determinants of health.

Social determinants of health include income and economic stability, access to quality education, access to healthcare, characteristics of the built environment (including housing quality, neighborhood safety, and availability of healthy foods), and social context (including social support, experiences of discrimination, and community cohesion). Addressing these upstream determinants requires action across multiple sectors, including education, housing, employment, agriculture, transportation, and urban planning, as well as within the health sector.

Global health governance has evolved significantly over recent decades, with the establishment of new financing mechanisms, normative frameworks, and partnerships aimed at improving health in low- and middle-income countries. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, Gavi the Vaccine Alliance, the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR), the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, and many other bilateral and multilateral actors have mobilized substantial resources and technical assistance for global health. These efforts have contributed to remarkable improvements in health outcomes in many low- and middle-income countries over the past two decades.

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted by United Nations member states in 2015, set a comprehensive global agenda for health and development through 2030. SDG 3 specifically calls for ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all at all ages, with specific targets related to maternal and child mortality, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, mental health, universal health coverage, and health workforce. Progress toward the SDGs has been uneven, and the COVID-19 pandemic has set back progress across many indicators, underscoring the need for renewed commitment and accelerated action.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a cross-cutting threat that affects the prevention and treatment of a wide range of infectious diseases. The overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human health, veterinary medicine, and agriculture has accelerated the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which are increasingly rendering standard treatments ineffective. AMR is estimated to cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year directly and contributes to millions more deaths when infections that would previously have been easily treated become life-threatening. Addressing AMR requires a One Health approach that recognizes the interconnections between human health, animal health, and environmental health, and encompasses stewardship of existing antimicrobials, development of new ones, strengthening of infection prevention and control, and international cooperation on surveillance and policy.

Research and innovation are essential for continued progress in global health. Investment in basic biomedical research, clinical trials, health systems research, and implementation science is needed to generate new knowledge, develop better tools, and identify the most effective and cost-effective ways to deliver existing interventions at scale. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared — including through mechanisms that facilitate access to medicines and vaccines in low- and middle-income countries — is a fundamental obligation of global health justice.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital health tools and telemedicine, creating new opportunities to extend the reach of health services, improve the continuity of care, and engage patients in their own health management. Ensuring that the benefits of digital health are equitably accessible — and do not exacerbate existing health disparities due to differential access to technology and digital literacy — is an important challenge for health policymakers and implementers.

Strengthening primary health care is recognized as the foundation of universal health coverage and an essential strategy for addressing health inequities. Strong primary health care systems provide comprehensive, accessible, people-centered care close to where people live and work, integrating health promotion, disease prevention, diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care. Community health workers — lay members of the community trained to provide a range of basic health services — play a vital role in extending the reach of primary health care to the most underserved populations.

International health regulations and global health security frameworks provide the legal and institutional foundation for collective action on cross-border health threats. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), administered by the WHO, require countries to develop and maintain core public health capacities for detecting, assessing, notifying, and responding to public health events of international concern. Strengthening these capacities, particularly in low-income countries, is a priority for global health security and for ensuring that the world is better prepared for future health crises.
