MATERNAL AND CHILD HEALTH: GLOBAL STATUS, CHALLENGES, AND INTERVENTIONS
Source: World Health Organization (WHO), UNICEF, and World Bank

INTRODUCTION

Maternal and child health encompasses the health of women during pregnancy, childbirth, and the postnatal period, as well as the health of children from birth through adolescence. The health of mothers and children is deeply interconnected: a healthy mother gives birth to a healthier child, and a child who survives and thrives in early life has a much better chance of growing into a healthy and productive adult. Investments in maternal and child health therefore yield compounding returns across generations and are foundational to sustainable human development.

Despite remarkable progress over recent decades, maternal and child mortality remain major global public health challenges. Every year, approximately 295,000 women die from complications of pregnancy or childbirth, and over 5 million children under the age of five die — the vast majority from causes that are preventable with existing, cost-effective interventions. The stark disparities in maternal and child health between rich and poor countries represent one of the greatest injustices in global health.

MATERNAL MORTALITY

Maternal mortality — defined as the death of a woman while pregnant or within 42 days of termination of pregnancy, from causes related to or aggravated by the pregnancy or its management — is one of the most sensitive indicators of a country's health system strength and the status of women in society. The global maternal mortality ratio (MMR) — the number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births — fell by approximately 34 percent between 2000 and 2020, from 339 to 223 deaths per 100,000 live births globally. While this represents meaningful progress, the numbers remain unacceptably high, and progress has been far slower than needed to meet global targets.

Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for approximately 70 percent of all maternal deaths globally. The lifetime risk of maternal death in low-income countries is dramatically higher than in high-income countries. A woman in sub-Saharan Africa has a 1 in 40 lifetime risk of dying from maternal causes, compared to 1 in 4,900 in high-income countries. These disparities reflect profound differences in access to quality antenatal care, skilled birth attendance, emergency obstetric care, and postnatal care.

The main direct causes of maternal death include major hemorrhage (bleeding), infections (sepsis), high blood pressure disorders of pregnancy (preeclampsia and eclampsia), unsafe abortion, and obstructed labor. Indirect causes — pre-existing medical conditions worsened by pregnancy — are increasingly recognized as important contributors to maternal mortality, including malaria, HIV/AIDS, and cardiac disease.

ANTENATAL CARE

Antenatal care (ANC) — the care provided by skilled healthcare providers to pregnant women and adolescent girls — is a critical opportunity to promote health during pregnancy, prevent complications, and prepare women and their families for labor, delivery, and the postnatal period. Recommended components of ANC include assessment of nutritional status and provision of iron and folic acid supplementation; screening for and management of infectious diseases (including malaria, HIV, syphilis, and tuberculosis); monitoring of fetal growth and wellbeing; screening for and management of hypertension, diabetes, and other medical complications; counseling on warning signs, birth preparedness, and breastfeeding; and provision of tetanus toxoid vaccination.

The WHO recommends a minimum of eight antenatal contacts during pregnancy, with the first contact in the first trimester. Globally, about 58 percent of women make the minimum recommended number of ANC visits, though coverage varies enormously by region and country. In sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia, many women receive fewer than the recommended number of ANC visits or receive no ANC at all.

SKILLED BIRTH ATTENDANCE AND DELIVERY CARE

The presence of a skilled health professional (doctor, midwife, or nurse with midwifery skills) at delivery is critical for preventing and managing potentially life-threatening complications during childbirth. Globally, approximately 81 percent of births are attended by skilled health personnel, but this proportion falls to below 50 percent in some low-income countries. Reducing the large proportion of births occurring outside health facilities without skilled attendance is a major priority for reducing maternal and neonatal mortality.

Emergency obstetric care (EmOC) is essential for managing life-threatening obstetric complications. Basic EmOC includes administration of oxytocin for postpartum hemorrhage and for induction of labor, administration of antibiotics for infection, administration of anticonvulsants for preeclampsia and eclampsia, manual removal of the placenta, removal of retained products of conception, assisted vaginal delivery (forceps or vacuum extraction), and neonatal resuscitation. Comprehensive EmOC adds surgery (including cesarean section) and blood transfusion. Access to quality EmOC in a timely manner — the concept of ensuring women can reach care within two hours of a life-threatening emergency — can dramatically reduce maternal and perinatal mortality.

NEONATAL HEALTH

The neonatal period (the first 28 days of life) is the most critical and vulnerable period in a child's life. Approximately 2.4 million newborns die each year during the first 28 days of life — an average of about 6,500 newborns per day. The leading causes of neonatal death are preterm birth complications, birth asphyxia (lack of oxygen at birth), and infections (including sepsis, pneumonia, and meningitis). Low birth weight — whether due to preterm birth or fetal growth restriction — is a major predictor of neonatal mortality and of long-term health outcomes including increased risk of chronic disease in adulthood.

Essential newborn care practices that can dramatically reduce neonatal mortality include immediate and thorough drying and stimulation of the newborn after birth; provision of thermal care (preventing hypothermia by promoting skin-to-skin contact between mother and baby, delayed bathing, and use of a warm environment); early initiation of breastfeeding within the first hour of life; cord care (clean cutting and clamping, application of chlorhexidine gel to the cord stump in high-mortality settings); eye care to prevent neonatal conjunctivitis; and newborn resuscitation for babies who do not breathe spontaneously at birth.

CHILD NUTRITION AND GROWTH

The first 1,000 days — from conception to a child's second birthday — represent a critical window of opportunity in which adequate nutrition, stimulation, and care can have lifelong effects on a child's health, cognitive development, and life potential. Conversely, malnutrition during this period can lead to stunting, wasting, and micronutrient deficiencies that have irreversible effects on brain development and physical growth.

Optimal infant and young child feeding (IYCF) practices are central to promoting child health and nutrition. These include initiation of breastfeeding within one hour of birth; exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life; introduction of safe, nutritious, and age-appropriate complementary foods at 6 months while continuing to breastfeed for 2 years or beyond; and provision of vitamin A supplementation and other micronutrients as recommended. Globally, promotion of optimal IYCF practices remains a major priority, as only about 44 percent of infants under 6 months are exclusively breastfed and the quality of complementary feeding remains inadequate in many low- and middle-income countries.

CHILDHOOD IMMUNIZATION AND DISEASE PREVENTION

Immunization is one of the most cost-effective tools for improving child health and survival. A comprehensive childhood immunization program protects children from a wide range of serious and potentially fatal infectious diseases including measles, diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio, Haemophilus influenzae type b disease, pneumococcal disease, rotavirus gastroenteritis, hepatitis B, and yellow fever.

In addition to immunization, other important child health interventions include oral rehydration therapy and zinc supplementation for diarrheal disease; antibiotics for pneumonia; insecticide-treated bed nets and artemisinin-based combination therapy for malaria; deworming; vitamin A supplementation; and access to safe water and sanitation facilities.

ADOLESCENT HEALTH

Adolescence (ages 10–19) is a pivotal period for health and development. Many health behaviors and conditions that affect adult health are established or begin during adolescence. Key health issues for adolescents globally include reproductive and sexual health (including early pregnancy and sexually transmitted infections); nutrition (including both undernutrition and overnutrition); mental health (depression, anxiety, and suicide are leading causes of adolescent morbidity and mortality); substance use (tobacco, alcohol, and drugs); injuries and violence; and non-communicable diseases.

Adolescent girls face particular health challenges, including early marriage and pregnancy, gender-based violence, and limited access to education and healthcare. Globally, complications of pregnancy and childbirth are the leading cause of death for girls aged 15–19. Investing in girls' education, delaying first pregnancy until at least age 18, providing access to family planning, and addressing gender inequality are among the most powerful strategies for improving adolescent girl health.

SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT GOALS AND MATERNAL-CHILD HEALTH

Improving maternal and child health is central to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). SDG 3 sets specific targets to reduce the global maternal mortality ratio to less than 70 per 100,000 live births, end preventable deaths of newborns and children under five, and ensure universal access to sexual and reproductive healthcare services by 2030. Achieving these ambitious targets will require accelerated action, sustained investment, and innovation in reaching the most marginalized and underserved populations.

CONCLUSION

The health of mothers and children is a fundamental measure of the health and equity of a society. While remarkable progress has been made over recent decades in reducing maternal and child mortality and improving nutrition and development, the numbers of preventable deaths remain unacceptably high and the disparities between rich and poor countries are stark. Achieving lasting improvements in maternal and child health requires strengthening health systems, expanding access to quality care, empowering women and girls, addressing social determinants of health, and ensuring sustained political commitment and investment at global, national, and community levels.

ADDITIONAL CONTEXT AND EMERGING RESEARCH

The global health landscape continues to evolve as new research, technologies, and interventions reshape our understanding and management of this health domain. Advances in genomics and precision medicine are enabling more personalized approaches to prevention, diagnosis, and treatment, tailoring interventions to the unique biological, social, and environmental characteristics of individuals and populations. Digital health technologies — including artificial intelligence, machine learning, mobile health applications, telemedicine, and electronic health records — are transforming the delivery of care and expanding access to health services in previously underserved settings.

Research continues to illuminate the complex relationships between this health condition and other aspects of health and well-being. The interconnections between physical and mental health, the role of the gut microbiome in disease risk and progression, the influence of environmental exposures and the built environment, and the lasting effects of adverse childhood experiences are all areas of active investigation that are yielding important new insights with implications for prevention and treatment.

The COVID-19 pandemic has had profound and lasting effects on global health systems, disease surveillance, health behaviors, and the mental and physical health of populations worldwide. For many health conditions, the pandemic led to disruptions in care, increases in undiagnosed cases, delays in treatment, and worsening of outcomes that will take years or decades to fully understand and address. At the same time, the unprecedented scientific response to COVID-19 — including the rapid development of vaccines using novel mRNA technology, the acceleration of clinical trials, and the expansion of global research collaborations — has generated new knowledge, tools, and capabilities with broad applications across many areas of health.

Climate change represents an increasingly urgent threat to global health, with implications for the distribution and burden of many infectious and non-communicable diseases. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and more frequent and intense extreme weather events affect the geographic range and seasonality of vector-borne diseases, the safety and availability of food and water, air quality, and the mental health and displacement of vulnerable populations. Integrating climate considerations into health policy, planning, and practice is becoming an essential dimension of public health action.

Universal health coverage (UHC) — the goal of ensuring that all people can access the health services they need without facing financial hardship — is central to achieving equity in health outcomes globally. Achieving UHC requires strengthening primary health care, expanding the health workforce, improving access to essential medicines and technologies, and ensuring sustainable financing for health systems. International cooperation, global health governance, and shared commitment to health as a fundamental human right are essential foundations for making further progress against disease and improving health for all people worldwide.

Health literacy — the ability of individuals to understand and use health information to make informed decisions about their health — plays an important role in shaping health behaviors and outcomes. Low health literacy is associated with poorer management of chronic conditions, reduced uptake of preventive services, worse health outcomes, and higher healthcare costs. Improving health literacy at the individual and community level, and making health information more accessible and understandable to all, are important strategies for empowering people to take control of their health.

Community engagement and participatory approaches are increasingly recognized as essential for the success of public health programs and interventions. Programs that actively involve communities in the design, implementation, and evaluation of health initiatives are more likely to be culturally appropriate, acceptable, and sustainable. Partnering with community organizations, religious institutions, traditional healers, and other trusted community actors can enhance the reach and effectiveness of health programs, particularly in settings where formal healthcare systems have limited reach or are viewed with mistrust.

The health workforce — doctors, nurses, midwives, community health workers, laboratory scientists, and other health professionals — is the backbone of any health system. Globally, there is a critical shortage of health workers, estimated at approximately 18 million by 2030, with the greatest shortfalls in low- and middle-income countries. Investing in health workforce education, training, and retention; ensuring fair compensation and safe working conditions; and developing innovative models of care that maximize the contributions of all types of health workers are essential priorities for strengthening health systems and improving health outcomes globally.

HEALTH EQUITY, SOCIAL DETERMINANTS, AND GLOBAL POLICY

The burden of disease is not distributed equally across populations. Individuals living in poverty, those with limited educational opportunities, racial and ethnic minorities, residents of rural and remote areas, refugees and displaced persons, and other marginalized groups consistently experience higher rates of illness, disability, and premature death compared to more advantaged populations. These disparities in health outcomes reflect systemic inequities in the social, economic, and environmental conditions that shape health — conditions often referred to as the social determinants of health.

Social determinants of health include income and economic stability, access to quality education, access to healthcare, characteristics of the built environment (including housing quality, neighborhood safety, and availability of healthy foods), and social context (including social support, experiences of discrimination, and community cohesion). Addressing these upstream determinants requires action across multiple sectors, including education, housing, employment, agriculture, transportation, and urban planning, as well as within the health sector.

Global health governance has evolved significantly over recent decades, with the establishment of new financing mechanisms, normative frameworks, and partnerships aimed at improving health in low- and middle-income countries. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, Gavi the Vaccine Alliance, the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR), the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, and many other bilateral and multilateral actors have mobilized substantial resources and technical assistance for global health. These efforts have contributed to remarkable improvements in health outcomes in many low- and middle-income countries over the past two decades.

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted by United Nations member states in 2015, set a comprehensive global agenda for health and development through 2030. SDG 3 specifically calls for ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all at all ages, with specific targets related to maternal and child mortality, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, mental health, universal health coverage, and health workforce. Progress toward the SDGs has been uneven, and the COVID-19 pandemic has set back progress across many indicators, underscoring the need for renewed commitment and accelerated action.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a cross-cutting threat that affects the prevention and treatment of a wide range of infectious diseases. The overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human health, veterinary medicine, and agriculture has accelerated the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which are increasingly rendering standard treatments ineffective. AMR is estimated to cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year directly and contributes to millions more deaths when infections that would previously have been easily treated become life-threatening. Addressing AMR requires a One Health approach that recognizes the interconnections between human health, animal health, and environmental health, and encompasses stewardship of existing antimicrobials, development of new ones, strengthening of infection prevention and control, and international cooperation on surveillance and policy.

Research and innovation are essential for continued progress in global health. Investment in basic biomedical research, clinical trials, health systems research, and implementation science is needed to generate new knowledge, develop better tools, and identify the most effective and cost-effective ways to deliver existing interventions at scale. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared — including through mechanisms that facilitate access to medicines and vaccines in low- and middle-income countries — is a fundamental obligation of global health justice.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital health tools and telemedicine, creating new opportunities to extend the reach of health services, improve the continuity of care, and engage patients in their own health management. Ensuring that the benefits of digital health are equitably accessible — and do not exacerbate existing health disparities due to differential access to technology and digital literacy — is an important challenge for health policymakers and implementers.

Strengthening primary health care is recognized as the foundation of universal health coverage and an essential strategy for addressing health inequities. Strong primary health care systems provide comprehensive, accessible, people-centered care close to where people live and work, integrating health promotion, disease prevention, diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care. Community health workers — lay members of the community trained to provide a range of basic health services — play a vital role in extending the reach of primary health care to the most underserved populations.

International health regulations and global health security frameworks provide the legal and institutional foundation for collective action on cross-border health threats. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), administered by the WHO, require countries to develop and maintain core public health capacities for detecting, assessing, notifying, and responding to public health events of international concern. Strengthening these capacities, particularly in low-income countries, is a priority for global health security and for ensuring that the world is better prepared for future health crises.
