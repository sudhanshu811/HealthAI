NUTRITION, OBESITY, AND GLOBAL DIETARY HEALTH
Source: World Health Organization (WHO), Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO)

INTRODUCTION

Nutrition is the process by which living organisms obtain and use food for growth, maintenance, and repair of body tissues. Good nutrition is fundamental to human health across all stages of life, from conception and infancy through adulthood and old age. Adequate nutrition supports physical growth and development, immune function, cognitive performance, reproductive health, and protection against chronic disease. Conversely, poor nutrition — whether due to undernutrition, micronutrient deficiency, or overnutrition — is a leading cause of illness, disability, and death worldwide.

The global nutrition landscape is characterized by what is termed a "double burden of malnutrition," in which undernutrition (including stunting, wasting, and micronutrient deficiencies) and overnutrition (overweight, obesity, and diet-related non-communicable diseases) coexist within the same populations and even within the same households. This dual burden poses complex challenges for public health nutrition policy and programs worldwide.

Obesity has emerged as one of the most serious global public health challenges of the 21st century. The World Health Organization defines overweight as a body mass index (BMI) of 25 or greater, and obesity as a BMI of 30 or greater. BMI is calculated as weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared (kg/m²). Since 1975, obesity has nearly tripled worldwide. Currently, more than 1 billion people worldwide are obese — 650 million adults, 340 million adolescents, and 39 million children. In 2022, approximately 2.5 billion adults aged 18 years and older were overweight. The epidemic of obesity is no longer confined to high-income countries; low- and middle-income countries are now experiencing rapid increases in overweight and obesity, particularly in urban areas.

UNDERNUTRITION AND MICRONUTRIENT DEFICIENCIES

Undernutrition refers to deficiencies, excesses, or imbalances in a person's intake of energy and nutrients. It encompasses stunting (low height for age, reflecting chronic malnutrition), wasting (low weight for height, reflecting acute malnutrition), underweight (low weight for age), and micronutrient deficiencies.

Despite significant progress over recent decades, undernutrition remains a major global health problem. An estimated 149 million children under five years of age are stunted, and 45 million are wasted. Undernutrition is a contributing factor in approximately 45 percent of deaths of children under five years of age. Stunting is associated with impaired cognitive development, reduced educational attainment, lower economic productivity, and increased risk of chronic disease in adulthood.

Micronutrient deficiencies, often called "hidden hunger," affect billions of people worldwide and can have serious health consequences even in the absence of visible signs of malnutrition. Key micronutrient deficiencies include iron deficiency, which is the most widespread micronutrient deficiency globally and causes iron-deficiency anemia, affecting cognitive development and work capacity; iodine deficiency, which causes goiter, cretinism, and impaired cognitive development; vitamin A deficiency, which causes night blindness, increased susceptibility to infections, and is a major cause of preventable blindness in children; and zinc deficiency, which impairs immune function, growth, and cognitive development.

OVERWEIGHT, OBESITY, AND DIET-RELATED NON-COMMUNICABLE DISEASES

Overweight and obesity result from a chronic energy imbalance in which energy intake from food and beverages exceeds energy expenditure. This imbalance is influenced by both individual behaviors and the broader food and physical activity environment. The rapid rise in obesity prevalence globally reflects profound changes in the food environment, including increased availability, affordability, and marketing of energy-dense, nutrient-poor processed and ultra-processed foods, alongside decreasing opportunities for physical activity in occupational, transportation, and recreational domains.

Obesity is a major risk factor for a wide range of serious non-communicable diseases, including type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular diseases (heart disease and stroke), certain cancers (including endometrial, breast, ovarian, prostate, liver, gallbladder, kidney, and colon cancer), musculoskeletal disorders (including osteoarthritis), and sleep apnea. People with obesity also face significantly elevated risks of depression, anxiety, and other mental health conditions. The health consequences of obesity are not limited to adults; children and adolescents who are overweight or obese are at increased risk of obesity and its complications in adulthood, as well as experiencing immediate health impacts including high blood pressure, high cholesterol, type 2 diabetes, and psychological problems such as bullying and low self-esteem.

DIETARY PATTERNS AND HEALTHY EATING

Dietary patterns play a central role in shaping nutrition status and health outcomes. A healthy diet throughout life helps prevent all forms of malnutrition and a range of non-communicable diseases and conditions. Healthy dietary patterns are characterized by high consumption of vegetables and fruits, legumes (beans, lentils, and peas), whole grains, nuts, and unsaturated vegetable oils; moderate consumption of fish, seafood, eggs, and poultry; low consumption of red meat and processed meat; and very low consumption of added sugars, refined grains, sodium, and trans fats.

The Mediterranean diet, DASH (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) diet, and other plant-forward dietary patterns have been extensively studied and shown to reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, certain cancers, and overall mortality. Traditional food cultures in many regions, including East Asian, Latin American, and African diets, also feature many elements of healthy dietary patterns, though they are increasingly being displaced by processed and Western-style diets as part of the global nutrition transition.

The WHO's global dietary recommendations include consuming at least 400 grams (5 portions) of fruits and vegetables per day; limiting intake of free sugars to less than 10 percent of total energy intake, with additional health benefits from reducing to less than 5 percent; limiting total fat intake to less than 30 percent of total energy intake, with less than 10 percent from saturated fats and less than 1 percent from trans fats; and limiting salt intake to less than 5 grams per day (approximately 2 grams of sodium).

ULTRA-PROCESSED FOODS AND THE NUTRITION TRANSITION

The global nutrition transition — the shift from traditional diets toward diets high in ultra-processed foods, added sugars, unhealthy fats, and sodium — is a major driver of the global obesity and non-communicable disease epidemic. Ultra-processed foods are industrial formulations that contain little if any whole food and include a wide range of ingredients not typically found in home kitchens, such as artificial flavors, colors, sweeteners, emulsifiers, and other additives. Examples include carbonated soft drinks, packaged snacks and cookies, instant noodles, fast food, and processed meats.

Ultra-processed foods now account for a growing share of total energy intake in many countries, including in low- and middle-income countries. A growing body of research links high consumption of ultra-processed foods with increased risk of obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, certain cancers, and even mental health disorders. The food industry's aggressive marketing of these products, particularly to children, is a major public health concern that has prompted calls for stronger regulatory action.

BREASTFEEDING AND INFANT NUTRITION

Breastfeeding is one of the most effective strategies for ensuring child health and survival. Breast milk is the ideal food for newborns and infants, providing all the nutrients they need for healthy development, as well as antibodies that help protect against common childhood illnesses such as diarrhea and pneumonia. The WHO recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life, followed by continued breastfeeding along with appropriate, safe, and adequate complementary foods until 2 years of age or beyond.

Globally, only about 44 percent of infants under 6 months of age are exclusively breastfed. Increasing breastfeeding rates is a global priority, as suboptimal breastfeeding is associated with increased infant and child mortality, increased risk of childhood infections and malnutrition, reduced cognitive development, and increased risk of obesity and non-communicable diseases in later life.

FOOD SECURITY AND THE RIGHT TO FOOD

Food security exists when all people, at all times, have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food that meets their dietary needs and food preferences for an active and healthy life. Food insecurity — the lack of reliable access to sufficient affordable, nutritious food — is both a cause and consequence of poverty and is closely linked to undernutrition, poor health, and reduced productivity.

Globally, an estimated 733 million people faced hunger in 2023, and 2.33 billion people experienced moderate or severe food insecurity. Food insecurity is driven by a complex interplay of factors including poverty, conflict, climate change and extreme weather events, economic inequality, weak governance, and disruptions to food supply chains. The COVID-19 pandemic and the conflict in Ukraine have both contributed to recent increases in global food insecurity.

POLICIES AND INTERVENTIONS

Addressing the double burden of malnutrition requires a comprehensive, multi-sectoral approach involving agriculture, food systems, health, education, social protection, water and sanitation, and trade and economic policy. Effective nutrition interventions include promotion of breastfeeding and complementary feeding; micronutrient supplementation and food fortification programs; school feeding programs; nutrition education and counseling; regulatory measures to restrict marketing of unhealthy foods to children; food labeling requirements; fiscal policies such as taxes on sugary drinks and subsidies on healthy foods; and policies to improve food system sustainability and resilience.

The WHO and partner agencies work with governments, civil society, and the private sector to develop and implement national nutrition policies and strategies that address all forms of malnutrition while promoting sustainable food systems.

CONCLUSION

Good nutrition is essential for health, well-being, and human potential at every stage of life. Despite remarkable progress in reducing undernutrition in many parts of the world, the global burden of malnutrition in all its forms remains enormous and is compounded by the rapidly rising epidemic of overweight, obesity, and diet-related non-communicable diseases. Transforming food systems to provide all people with access to safe, nutritious, and sustainable diets is both a moral imperative and one of the most important investments the world can make in human health, economic development, and environmental sustainability.

ADDITIONAL CONTEXT AND EMERGING RESEARCH

The global health landscape continues to evolve as new research, technologies, and interventions reshape our understanding and management of this health domain. Advances in genomics and precision medicine are enabling more personalized approaches to prevention, diagnosis, and treatment, tailoring interventions to the unique biological, social, and environmental characteristics of individuals and populations. Digital health technologies — including artificial intelligence, machine learning, mobile health applications, telemedicine, and electronic health records — are transforming the delivery of care and expanding access to health services in previously underserved settings.

Research continues to illuminate the complex relationships between this health condition and other aspects of health and well-being. The interconnections between physical and mental health, the role of the gut microbiome in disease risk and progression, the influence of environmental exposures and the built environment, and the lasting effects of adverse childhood experiences are all areas of active investigation that are yielding important new insights with implications for prevention and treatment.

The COVID-19 pandemic has had profound and lasting effects on global health systems, disease surveillance, health behaviors, and the mental and physical health of populations worldwide. For many health conditions, the pandemic led to disruptions in care, increases in undiagnosed cases, delays in treatment, and worsening of outcomes that will take years or decades to fully understand and address. At the same time, the unprecedented scientific response to COVID-19 — including the rapid development of vaccines using novel mRNA technology, the acceleration of clinical trials, and the expansion of global research collaborations — has generated new knowledge, tools, and capabilities with broad applications across many areas of health.

Climate change represents an increasingly urgent threat to global health, with implications for the distribution and burden of many infectious and non-communicable diseases. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and more frequent and intense extreme weather events affect the geographic range and seasonality of vector-borne diseases, the safety and availability of food and water, air quality, and the mental health and displacement of vulnerable populations. Integrating climate considerations into health policy, planning, and practice is becoming an essential dimension of public health action.

Universal health coverage (UHC) — the goal of ensuring that all people can access the health services they need without facing financial hardship — is central to achieving equity in health outcomes globally. Achieving UHC requires strengthening primary health care, expanding the health workforce, improving access to essential medicines and technologies, and ensuring sustainable financing for health systems. International cooperation, global health governance, and shared commitment to health as a fundamental human right are essential foundations for making further progress against disease and improving health for all people worldwide.

Health literacy — the ability of individuals to understand and use health information to make informed decisions about their health — plays an important role in shaping health behaviors and outcomes. Low health literacy is associated with poorer management of chronic conditions, reduced uptake of preventive services, worse health outcomes, and higher healthcare costs. Improving health literacy at the individual and community level, and making health information more accessible and understandable to all, are important strategies for empowering people to take control of their health.

Community engagement and participatory approaches are increasingly recognized as essential for the success of public health programs and interventions. Programs that actively involve communities in the design, implementation, and evaluation of health initiatives are more likely to be culturally appropriate, acceptable, and sustainable. Partnering with community organizations, religious institutions, traditional healers, and other trusted community actors can enhance the reach and effectiveness of health programs, particularly in settings where formal healthcare systems have limited reach or are viewed with mistrust.

The health workforce — doctors, nurses, midwives, community health workers, laboratory scientists, and other health professionals — is the backbone of any health system. Globally, there is a critical shortage of health workers, estimated at approximately 18 million by 2030, with the greatest shortfalls in low- and middle-income countries. Investing in health workforce education, training, and retention; ensuring fair compensation and safe working conditions; and developing innovative models of care that maximize the contributions of all types of health workers are essential priorities for strengthening health systems and improving health outcomes globally.

HEALTH EQUITY, SOCIAL DETERMINANTS, AND GLOBAL POLICY

The burden of disease is not distributed equally across populations. Individuals living in poverty, those with limited educational opportunities, racial and ethnic minorities, residents of rural and remote areas, refugees and displaced persons, and other marginalized groups consistently experience higher rates of illness, disability, and premature death compared to more advantaged populations. These disparities in health outcomes reflect systemic inequities in the social, economic, and environmental conditions that shape health — conditions often referred to as the social determinants of health.

Social determinants of health include income and economic stability, access to quality education, access to healthcare, characteristics of the built environment (including housing quality, neighborhood safety, and availability of healthy foods), and social context (including social support, experiences of discrimination, and community cohesion). Addressing these upstream determinants requires action across multiple sectors, including education, housing, employment, agriculture, transportation, and urban planning, as well as within the health sector.

Global health governance has evolved significantly over recent decades, with the establishment of new financing mechanisms, normative frameworks, and partnerships aimed at improving health in low- and middle-income countries. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, Gavi the Vaccine Alliance, the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR), the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, and many other bilateral and multilateral actors have mobilized substantial resources and technical assistance for global health. These efforts have contributed to remarkable improvements in health outcomes in many low- and middle-income countries over the past two decades.

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted by United Nations member states in 2015, set a comprehensive global agenda for health and development through 2030. SDG 3 specifically calls for ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all at all ages, with specific targets related to maternal and child mortality, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, mental health, universal health coverage, and health workforce. Progress toward the SDGs has been uneven, and the COVID-19 pandemic has set back progress across many indicators, underscoring the need for renewed commitment and accelerated action.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a cross-cutting threat that affects the prevention and treatment of a wide range of infectious diseases. The overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human health, veterinary medicine, and agriculture has accelerated the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which are increasingly rendering standard treatments ineffective. AMR is estimated to cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year directly and contributes to millions more deaths when infections that would previously have been easily treated become life-threatening. Addressing AMR requires a One Health approach that recognizes the interconnections between human health, animal health, and environmental health, and encompasses stewardship of existing antimicrobials, development of new ones, strengthening of infection prevention and control, and international cooperation on surveillance and policy.

Research and innovation are essential for continued progress in global health. Investment in basic biomedical research, clinical trials, health systems research, and implementation science is needed to generate new knowledge, develop better tools, and identify the most effective and cost-effective ways to deliver existing interventions at scale. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared — including through mechanisms that facilitate access to medicines and vaccines in low- and middle-income countries — is a fundamental obligation of global health justice.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital health tools and telemedicine, creating new opportunities to extend the reach of health services, improve the continuity of care, and engage patients in their own health management. Ensuring that the benefits of digital health are equitably accessible — and do not exacerbate existing health disparities due to differential access to technology and digital literacy — is an important challenge for health policymakers and implementers.

Strengthening primary health care is recognized as the foundation of universal health coverage and an essential strategy for addressing health inequities. Strong primary health care systems provide comprehensive, accessible, people-centered care close to where people live and work, integrating health promotion, disease prevention, diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care. Community health workers — lay members of the community trained to provide a range of basic health services — play a vital role in extending the reach of primary health care to the most underserved populations.

International health regulations and global health security frameworks provide the legal and institutional foundation for collective action on cross-border health threats. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), administered by the WHO, require countries to develop and maintain core public health capacities for detecting, assessing, notifying, and responding to public health events of international concern. Strengthening these capacities, particularly in low-income countries, is a priority for global health security and for ensuring that the world is better prepared for future health crises.
