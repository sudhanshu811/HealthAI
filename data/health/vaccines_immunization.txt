VACCINES AND IMMUNIZATION: SCIENCE, IMPACT, AND GLOBAL PROGRAMS
Source: World Health Organization (WHO), UNICEF, and Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance

INTRODUCTION

Vaccination is one of the most successful and cost-effective public health interventions in history. Vaccines work by stimulating the immune system to produce a protective immune response against specific pathogens — bacteria, viruses, or their toxins — without causing the disease itself. This immune memory allows vaccinated individuals to mount a rapid and effective defense if they are subsequently exposed to the pathogen, preventing illness or reducing its severity.

The impact of vaccination on global health is profound. Vaccines have contributed to the eradication of smallpox — the only human disease to have been eradicated — the near-eradication of polio, and dramatic reductions in the incidence and mortality of many serious infectious diseases including measles, diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, hepatitis B, and Haemophilus influenzae type b meningitis. The WHO estimates that vaccines currently prevent between 3.5 and 5 million deaths per year. A 2021 analysis estimated that vaccination programs delivered between 2000 and 2030 in low- and middle-income countries will have averted approximately 69 million child deaths.

HOW VACCINES WORK

The immune system defends the body against infection through two main mechanisms: innate immunity, which provides a rapid, non-specific first-line defense against pathogens, and adaptive immunity, which mounts a targeted, specific response against particular pathogens and generates immunological memory.

Vaccines harness the adaptive immune system. When a vaccine is administered, it introduces antigens — substances that the immune system recognizes as foreign — derived from the target pathogen. This stimulates the adaptive immune response, including the production of antigen-specific antibodies by B lymphocytes and the activation of T lymphocytes. Crucially, some of these activated B and T cells differentiate into long-lived memory cells that persist after the infection or vaccination is resolved. Upon subsequent exposure to the same pathogen, these memory cells enable a faster and stronger immune response that can neutralize the pathogen before it causes significant disease.

The level of protective immunity induced by vaccination varies with the vaccine type, the number of doses given, the characteristics of the pathogen, and individual factors such as age, nutritional status, and immune competence. Some vaccines provide lifelong protection after a single dose (such as the measles vaccine), while others require multiple doses and periodic booster doses (such as the tetanus vaccine).

TYPES OF VACCINES

Vaccines can be categorized based on the type of antigen they contain and the technology used to produce them.

Live-Attenuated Vaccines: Live-attenuated vaccines contain weakened (attenuated) forms of the live pathogen that are capable of replicating but do not cause disease in healthy individuals. Because they closely mimic natural infection, live-attenuated vaccines typically produce strong and long-lasting immune responses, often with only one or two doses. Examples include the measles-mumps-rubella (MMR) vaccine, the chickenpox (varicella) vaccine, the yellow fever vaccine, and the oral polio vaccine. The main limitations of live-attenuated vaccines are that they require cold chain storage, and they can rarely cause disease in immunocompromised individuals.

Inactivated Vaccines: Inactivated vaccines contain killed or chemically inactivated pathogens. Because the pathogen cannot replicate, inactivated vaccines tend to produce weaker immune responses than live-attenuated vaccines and typically require multiple doses and booster doses. Examples include the inactivated polio vaccine (IPV), the whole-cell pertussis vaccine, and influenza vaccines. Inactivated vaccines are generally more stable than live-attenuated vaccines.

Subunit, Recombinant, and Conjugate Vaccines: These vaccines contain purified components of the pathogen, such as proteins or polysaccharides, that are recognized by the immune system as antigens. They are highly specific and safe but often require adjuvants (immune-stimulating substances) and multiple doses to generate adequate immunity. Examples include the hepatitis B vaccine (which uses a recombinant hepatitis B surface antigen), the pneumococcal conjugate vaccine (which uses capsular polysaccharides conjugated to a carrier protein), the Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) vaccine, and the human papillomavirus (HPV) vaccine.

Toxoid Vaccines: Toxoid vaccines contain inactivated forms of bacterial toxins (toxoids) that are responsible for causing disease. They protect against the harmful effects of the toxin rather than against the bacterium itself. Examples include the diphtheria and tetanus toxoids, which are components of the widely used DTP (diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis) combination vaccine.

mRNA Vaccines: mRNA vaccines are a newer vaccine technology that delivers messenger RNA encoding an antigen of the pathogen directly into human cells. The cells then produce the antigen, which stimulates an immune response. mRNA vaccines do not contain live pathogen, do not interact with the cell's DNA, and are rapidly produced. The first widely used mRNA vaccines were developed for COVID-19 (the Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna vaccines). mRNA vaccine technology holds great promise for future vaccines against a range of infectious diseases and potentially cancers.

THE EXPANDED PROGRAMME ON IMMUNIZATION

The WHO's Expanded Programme on Immunization (EPI), established in 1974, has been central to the dramatic improvements in global immunization coverage achieved over the past five decades. The EPI works with national governments and partners to ensure the availability, accessibility, and quality of immunization services worldwide. Today, the routine childhood immunization schedule recommended by WHO includes vaccines against tuberculosis (BCG), hepatitis B, polio, diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, Haemophilus influenzae type b, pneumococcal disease, rotavirus, measles, and rubella, with additional vaccines (such as HPV, typhoid, and meningococcal vaccines) recommended for specific populations and settings.

Global immunization coverage has increased substantially since the EPI was established. The coverage with three doses of diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis (DTP3) vaccine — a commonly used indicator of immunization system performance — increased from below 5 percent globally in 1974 to approximately 84 percent in 2022. However, global immunization coverage has not returned to pre-COVID-19 pandemic levels. The pandemic severely disrupted immunization services worldwide, leading to significant declines in coverage and leaving millions of children unvaccinated. WHO and UNICEF have reported that the number of children who did not receive a single dose of DTP vaccine (so-called "zero-dose" children) increased significantly during the pandemic, raising the risk of outbreaks of vaccine-preventable diseases.

HERD IMMUNITY

Herd immunity (also called community immunity or population immunity) occurs when a sufficiently large proportion of a population has become immune to an infectious disease — either through vaccination or prior infection — that the spread of the disease is limited and even unvaccinated individuals are indirectly protected. The threshold for herd immunity varies by disease depending on how contagious it is (measured by the basic reproduction number, R0). For measles, one of the most contagious infectious diseases, herd immunity requires approximately 95 percent of the population to be immune. For polio, the threshold is estimated at 80 to 85 percent. Herd immunity is particularly important for protecting individuals who cannot be vaccinated due to age, medical contraindications, or immune deficiency.

VACCINE SAFETY

Vaccines undergo rigorous safety evaluation before licensure and continue to be monitored for safety after approval and deployment. All licensed vaccines are extensively tested in clinical trials for safety, immunogenicity (ability to stimulate an immune response), and efficacy before being approved by regulatory agencies. After licensure, post-marketing pharmacovigilance systems continuously monitor vaccines for adverse events in the general population, which may be too rare to detect in pre-licensure trials.

Like all medical interventions, vaccines can cause side effects. Most vaccine side effects are mild and temporary, such as soreness at the injection site, low-grade fever, or fatigue. Serious adverse events are rare but are carefully investigated. The benefits of vaccines in preventing serious illness and death substantially outweigh the risks of adverse events for the vast majority of people. The scientific consensus, based on decades of research and the monitoring of billions of doses administered worldwide, is that vaccines are safe, effective, and essential for protecting public health.

Vaccine hesitancy — the reluctance or refusal to vaccinate despite the availability of vaccines — has been identified by WHO as one of the top ten threats to global health. Vaccine hesitancy is driven by complex factors including concerns about vaccine safety, distrust of healthcare systems or government, misinformation spread through social media and other channels, religious or philosophical beliefs, and complacency in settings where vaccine-preventable diseases are no longer commonly seen. Addressing vaccine hesitancy requires tailored communication strategies, engagement with communities and trusted messengers, and addressing the root causes of distrust.

THE IMMUNIZATION AGENDA 2030

The WHO's Immunization Agenda 2030 (IA2030) sets a global vision and strategy for immunization for the decade 2021 to 2030. IA2030 aims to leave no one behind by ensuring that everyone, everywhere, at every age benefits from vaccines. Key targets include achieving 90 percent national coverage with essential vaccines for every country, leaving fewer than 1 percent of districts below 50 percent coverage, and reducing by half the number of children who are unvaccinated. IA2030 also calls for the introduction of new and improved vaccines, the elimination and eradication of specific vaccine-preventable diseases, and the strengthening of immunization as a platform for primary health care and universal health coverage.

CONCLUSION

Vaccines are among the most powerful tools available to prevent infectious disease and save lives. The remarkable reductions in the incidence and mortality of many once-devastating diseases over the past century stand as testament to the transformative power of vaccination. Sustaining and expanding the gains of immunization requires continued investment in vaccine development and delivery, strong health systems, effective communication and community engagement, and unwavering political commitment to the goal of equitable immunization for all.

ADDITIONAL CONTEXT AND EMERGING RESEARCH

The global health landscape continues to evolve as new research, technologies, and interventions reshape our understanding and management of this health domain. Advances in genomics and precision medicine are enabling more personalized approaches to prevention, diagnosis, and treatment, tailoring interventions to the unique biological, social, and environmental characteristics of individuals and populations. Digital health technologies — including artificial intelligence, machine learning, mobile health applications, telemedicine, and electronic health records — are transforming the delivery of care and expanding access to health services in previously underserved settings.

Research continues to illuminate the complex relationships between this health condition and other aspects of health and well-being. The interconnections between physical and mental health, the role of the gut microbiome in disease risk and progression, the influence of environmental exposures and the built environment, and the lasting effects of adverse childhood experiences are all areas of active investigation that are yielding important new insights with implications for prevention and treatment.

The COVID-19 pandemic has had profound and lasting effects on global health systems, disease surveillance, health behaviors, and the mental and physical health of populations worldwide. For many health conditions, the pandemic led to disruptions in care, increases in undiagnosed cases, delays in treatment, and worsening of outcomes that will take years or decades to fully understand and address. At the same time, the unprecedented scientific response to COVID-19 — including the rapid development of vaccines using novel mRNA technology, the acceleration of clinical trials, and the expansion of global research collaborations — has generated new knowledge, tools, and capabilities with broad applications across many areas of health.

Climate change represents an increasingly urgent threat to global health, with implications for the distribution and burden of many infectious and non-communicable diseases. Rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and more frequent and intense extreme weather events affect the geographic range and seasonality of vector-borne diseases, the safety and availability of food and water, air quality, and the mental health and displacement of vulnerable populations. Integrating climate considerations into health policy, planning, and practice is becoming an essential dimension of public health action.

Universal health coverage (UHC) — the goal of ensuring that all people can access the health services they need without facing financial hardship — is central to achieving equity in health outcomes globally. Achieving UHC requires strengthening primary health care, expanding the health workforce, improving access to essential medicines and technologies, and ensuring sustainable financing for health systems. International cooperation, global health governance, and shared commitment to health as a fundamental human right are essential foundations for making further progress against disease and improving health for all people worldwide.

Health literacy — the ability of individuals to understand and use health information to make informed decisions about their health — plays an important role in shaping health behaviors and outcomes. Low health literacy is associated with poorer management of chronic conditions, reduced uptake of preventive services, worse health outcomes, and higher healthcare costs. Improving health literacy at the individual and community level, and making health information more accessible and understandable to all, are important strategies for empowering people to take control of their health.

Community engagement and participatory approaches are increasingly recognized as essential for the success of public health programs and interventions. Programs that actively involve communities in the design, implementation, and evaluation of health initiatives are more likely to be culturally appropriate, acceptable, and sustainable. Partnering with community organizations, religious institutions, traditional healers, and other trusted community actors can enhance the reach and effectiveness of health programs, particularly in settings where formal healthcare systems have limited reach or are viewed with mistrust.

The health workforce — doctors, nurses, midwives, community health workers, laboratory scientists, and other health professionals — is the backbone of any health system. Globally, there is a critical shortage of health workers, estimated at approximately 18 million by 2030, with the greatest shortfalls in low- and middle-income countries. Investing in health workforce education, training, and retention; ensuring fair compensation and safe working conditions; and developing innovative models of care that maximize the contributions of all types of health workers are essential priorities for strengthening health systems and improving health outcomes globally.

HEALTH EQUITY, SOCIAL DETERMINANTS, AND GLOBAL POLICY

The burden of disease is not distributed equally across populations. Individuals living in poverty, those with limited educational opportunities, racial and ethnic minorities, residents of rural and remote areas, refugees and displaced persons, and other marginalized groups consistently experience higher rates of illness, disability, and premature death compared to more advantaged populations. These disparities in health outcomes reflect systemic inequities in the social, economic, and environmental conditions that shape health — conditions often referred to as the social determinants of health.

Social determinants of health include income and economic stability, access to quality education, access to healthcare, characteristics of the built environment (including housing quality, neighborhood safety, and availability of healthy foods), and social context (including social support, experiences of discrimination, and community cohesion). Addressing these upstream determinants requires action across multiple sectors, including education, housing, employment, agriculture, transportation, and urban planning, as well as within the health sector.

Global health governance has evolved significantly over recent decades, with the establishment of new financing mechanisms, normative frameworks, and partnerships aimed at improving health in low- and middle-income countries. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria, Gavi the Vaccine Alliance, the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR), the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, and many other bilateral and multilateral actors have mobilized substantial resources and technical assistance for global health. These efforts have contributed to remarkable improvements in health outcomes in many low- and middle-income countries over the past two decades.

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted by United Nations member states in 2015, set a comprehensive global agenda for health and development through 2030. SDG 3 specifically calls for ensuring healthy lives and promoting well-being for all at all ages, with specific targets related to maternal and child mortality, infectious diseases, non-communicable diseases, mental health, universal health coverage, and health workforce. Progress toward the SDGs has been uneven, and the COVID-19 pandemic has set back progress across many indicators, underscoring the need for renewed commitment and accelerated action.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a cross-cutting threat that affects the prevention and treatment of a wide range of infectious diseases. The overuse and misuse of antibiotics in human health, veterinary medicine, and agriculture has accelerated the evolution of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, which are increasingly rendering standard treatments ineffective. AMR is estimated to cause approximately 1.27 million deaths per year directly and contributes to millions more deaths when infections that would previously have been easily treated become life-threatening. Addressing AMR requires a One Health approach that recognizes the interconnections between human health, animal health, and environmental health, and encompasses stewardship of existing antimicrobials, development of new ones, strengthening of infection prevention and control, and international cooperation on surveillance and policy.

Research and innovation are essential for continued progress in global health. Investment in basic biomedical research, clinical trials, health systems research, and implementation science is needed to generate new knowledge, develop better tools, and identify the most effective and cost-effective ways to deliver existing interventions at scale. Ensuring that the benefits of research are equitably shared — including through mechanisms that facilitate access to medicines and vaccines in low- and middle-income countries — is a fundamental obligation of global health justice.

The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of digital health tools and telemedicine, creating new opportunities to extend the reach of health services, improve the continuity of care, and engage patients in their own health management. Ensuring that the benefits of digital health are equitably accessible — and do not exacerbate existing health disparities due to differential access to technology and digital literacy — is an important challenge for health policymakers and implementers.

Strengthening primary health care is recognized as the foundation of universal health coverage and an essential strategy for addressing health inequities. Strong primary health care systems provide comprehensive, accessible, people-centered care close to where people live and work, integrating health promotion, disease prevention, diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care. Community health workers — lay members of the community trained to provide a range of basic health services — play a vital role in extending the reach of primary health care to the most underserved populations.

International health regulations and global health security frameworks provide the legal and institutional foundation for collective action on cross-border health threats. The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), administered by the WHO, require countries to develop and maintain core public health capacities for detecting, assessing, notifying, and responding to public health events of international concern. Strengthening these capacities, particularly in low-income countries, is a priority for global health security and for ensuring that the world is better prepared for future health crises.
