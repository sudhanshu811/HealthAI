DIABETES: A COMPREHENSIVE GLOBAL HEALTH OVERVIEW
Source: World Health Organization (WHO) and International Diabetes Federation (IDF)

INTRODUCTION

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disease characterized by elevated levels of blood glucose (or blood sugar), which leads over time to serious damage to the heart, blood vessels, eyes, kidneys, and nerves. It is one of the fastest-growing global health challenges of the 21st century. The disease affects people of all ages, nationalities, and socioeconomic backgrounds, making it a truly universal concern. As of recent estimates, over 422 million people worldwide have diabetes, and the number continues to rise at an alarming rate. The majority of people with diabetes live in low- and middle-income countries, where healthcare infrastructure is often insufficient to manage the long-term complications associated with the disease.

Diabetes was responsible for an estimated 1.5 million deaths directly in a recent year, with high blood sugar being responsible for an additional 2.2 million deaths by increasing the risks of cardiovascular and other diseases. The global economic burden of diabetes is also staggering, with healthcare spending on the disease estimated in the trillions of dollars annually, encompassing direct medical costs, lost productivity, and premature mortality.

TYPES OF DIABETES

There are several main forms of diabetes, each with distinct causes and mechanisms:

Type 1 Diabetes: Type 1 diabetes, previously known as insulin-dependent or juvenile-onset diabetes, is an autoimmune condition. In Type 1 diabetes, the body's immune system mistakenly attacks and destroys the insulin-producing beta cells of the pancreas. As a result, the body produces little to no insulin, a hormone essential for allowing glucose to enter cells and be used for energy. People with Type 1 diabetes require lifelong insulin therapy to survive. The exact cause of Type 1 diabetes remains unknown, but it is believed to involve a combination of genetic susceptibility and environmental triggers, such as viral infections. Type 1 diabetes accounts for approximately 5 to 10 percent of all diabetes cases and is commonly diagnosed in children and young adults, although it can occur at any age. Without proper insulin management, people with Type 1 diabetes develop dangerously high blood glucose levels that can lead to diabetic ketoacidosis, a life-threatening emergency.

Type 2 Diabetes: Type 2 diabetes is the most common form of diabetes, accounting for approximately 90 to 95 percent of all cases. In Type 2 diabetes, the body either does not produce enough insulin or does not effectively use the insulin it produces — a condition called insulin resistance. Over time, the pancreas may become unable to keep up with the increased demand for insulin, resulting in chronically elevated blood glucose levels. Type 2 diabetes is strongly associated with lifestyle factors, including physical inactivity, poor diet, overweight, and obesity. It is also influenced by genetic factors and is more common in older adults, although due to rising obesity rates, it is increasingly being diagnosed in children and adolescents. Unlike Type 1 diabetes, Type 2 diabetes can often be prevented or delayed through lifestyle changes such as achieving and maintaining a healthy weight, eating a balanced diet, and engaging in regular physical activity.

Gestational Diabetes: Gestational diabetes refers to elevated blood glucose levels that develop during pregnancy and are first detected at this time. It occurs because pregnancy-related hormones can impair the action of insulin. Most women with gestational diabetes do not have diabetes prior to pregnancy, and blood glucose usually returns to normal after delivery. However, gestational diabetes significantly increases the risk of complications during pregnancy and delivery. Women who have had gestational diabetes also have a higher risk of developing Type 2 diabetes later in life. Babies born to mothers with gestational diabetes are at increased risk of being large for gestational age, experiencing low blood sugar after birth, and developing obesity and Type 2 diabetes later in life.

Other Specific Types: In addition to the main forms described above, diabetes can also result from other specific causes, including genetic defects of the beta cell or insulin action, diseases of the exocrine pancreas such as pancreatitis and cystic fibrosis, drug- or chemical-induced diabetes, and other rare conditions. These forms collectively account for a small percentage of overall diabetes cases.

CAUSES AND RISK FACTORS

The causes and risk factors for diabetes vary depending on the type. For Type 1 diabetes, the primary cause is autoimmune destruction of the insulin-producing cells of the pancreas. Genetic factors play an important role, as certain HLA gene variants are associated with increased risk. Environmental triggers, including certain viral infections, may also play a role in initiating the autoimmune response in genetically susceptible individuals.

For Type 2 diabetes, the risk factors are much more closely linked to lifestyle and environmental conditions. The most significant risk factors include overweight and obesity, particularly abdominal obesity; physical inactivity; unhealthy diet rich in refined carbohydrates, added sugars, and saturated fats; family history of diabetes; increasing age; ethnicity — certain ethnic groups, including South Asian, East Asian, African, and Latino populations, are at higher risk; history of gestational diabetes; prediabetes — a condition of blood glucose levels higher than normal but not yet high enough for a diabetes diagnosis; high blood pressure; and abnormal cholesterol or triglyceride levels.

SYMPTOMS AND DIAGNOSIS

Diabetes can present with a range of symptoms, although many people, particularly those with Type 2 diabetes, may have the condition for years without realizing it. Common symptoms of diabetes include frequent urination, excessive thirst, unexplained weight loss, extreme hunger, blurred vision, slow-healing sores or frequent infections, fatigue, and tingling or numbness in the hands or feet.

Diabetes is diagnosed using blood tests that measure blood glucose levels. The main diagnostic tests include the fasting plasma glucose test, which measures blood glucose after an overnight fast; the oral glucose tolerance test, which measures blood glucose before and two hours after drinking a glucose-rich solution; the hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) test, which provides an average of blood glucose levels over the past two to three months; and the random plasma glucose test, which measures blood glucose at any time of day regardless of when the person last ate.

COMPLICATIONS

Uncontrolled diabetes leads to serious and wide-ranging complications affecting multiple organ systems. Cardiovascular disease is the most common cause of death among people with diabetes, and people with diabetes have a significantly higher risk of heart disease, stroke, and peripheral vascular disease. Diabetic neuropathy, or nerve damage, is one of the most prevalent complications, affecting up to 50 percent of people with diabetes and causing pain, tingling, loss of sensation, and in severe cases, amputation of the lower limbs. Diabetic nephropathy, or kidney disease, is a leading cause of kidney failure globally. Diabetic retinopathy is the leading cause of blindness in working-age adults in many countries, affecting the blood vessels in the retina. Other complications include increased susceptibility to infections, foot ulcers, dental problems, and psychological disorders such as depression and anxiety.

TREATMENT AND MANAGEMENT

Diabetes management depends on the type and severity of the condition. Type 1 diabetes requires lifelong insulin therapy, which may be administered via multiple daily injections or a continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion pump. Advances in technology, such as continuous glucose monitors and closed-loop insulin delivery systems (artificial pancreas), have significantly improved blood glucose management and quality of life for people with Type 1 diabetes.

Type 2 diabetes is managed primarily through lifestyle modifications, including dietary changes, increased physical activity, and weight loss. When lifestyle changes are insufficient to achieve target blood glucose levels, oral antidiabetic medications or injectable therapies may be prescribed. Metformin is typically the first-line medication for Type 2 diabetes. Other drug classes include sulfonylureas, SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, and DPP-4 inhibitors. Insulin may also be required for some people with Type 2 diabetes, particularly as the disease progresses.

Regardless of the type, effective diabetes management also involves regular monitoring of blood glucose levels, management of blood pressure and cholesterol levels, regular screening for complications, foot care, and psychological support.

PREVENTION

Type 1 diabetes cannot currently be prevented. However, Type 2 diabetes and gestational diabetes are largely preventable through lifestyle modifications. The WHO recommends maintaining a healthy body weight, engaging in regular physical activity of at least 30 minutes on most days of the week, eating a healthy diet that avoids sugar and saturated fats, and avoiding tobacco use. Several large-scale clinical trials, including the Diabetes Prevention Program in the United States and the Finnish Diabetes Prevention Study, have demonstrated that intensive lifestyle interventions can reduce the incidence of Type 2 diabetes by 58 percent or more in high-risk individuals.

GLOBAL BURDEN AND STATISTICS

The global burden of diabetes is substantial and continues to grow. According to WHO estimates, the number of people with diabetes has risen from 108 million in 1980 to over 422 million in 2014, and projections suggest that more than 700 million adults will have diabetes by 2045 if current trends continue. The prevalence of diabetes is increasing in low- and middle-income countries, where more than three-quarters of adults with diabetes live. Sub-Saharan Africa, the Middle East, and South-East Asia are expected to see the most rapid increases in the number of people with diabetes over the coming decades.

The economic cost of diabetes is enormous. Direct health expenditures on diabetes are estimated at 760 billion USD globally per year, accounting for approximately 10 percent of total adult healthcare spending. Indirect costs, including lost productivity due to disability and premature death, add substantially to this figure. The disease disproportionately affects working-age adults in many low- and middle-income countries, amplifying its economic impact.

DIABETES AND COVID-19

The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the complex relationship between diabetes and infectious diseases. People with diabetes are at significantly higher risk of severe illness and death from COVID-19 compared to those without diabetes. Hyperglycemia impairs immune function and increases susceptibility to infections, while COVID-19 itself can cause acute metabolic disturbances in people with diabetes. The pandemic has also disrupted diabetes care globally, with reduced access to healthcare, physical activity, and healthy food during lockdowns exacerbating the challenges of diabetes management.

CONCLUSION

Diabetes is one of the most significant and growing public health challenges of our time. Its high prevalence, serious complications, and enormous economic burden make it a priority for global health action. Effective prevention, early detection, and comprehensive management of diabetes require coordinated efforts from governments, healthcare systems, communities, and individuals. Investment in research, education, and accessible healthcare services is essential to turn the tide on this global epidemic and improve the quality of life for the hundreds of millions of people living with diabetes worldwide.

DIABETES TECHNOLOGY AND INNOVATION

The landscape of diabetes management has been transformed by rapid technological advances over the past decade. Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) systems allow people with diabetes to track their blood glucose levels in real time throughout the day and night, without the need for frequent fingerstick blood tests. CGM devices use a small sensor inserted under the skin to measure glucose levels in the interstitial fluid every few minutes, transmitting data wirelessly to a receiver, smartphone, or insulin pump. CGM enables detection of patterns, trends, and episodes of hypoglycemia (low blood glucose) or hyperglycemia (high blood glucose) that would be missed with traditional self-monitoring of blood glucose. This technology has been shown to improve glycemic control and quality of life in both Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes.

Insulin pump therapy (continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion, CSII) delivers insulin continuously through a small catheter inserted under the skin, allowing more flexible and precise insulin delivery than multiple daily injections. Modern insulin pumps can integrate with CGM systems to create "sensor-augmented pump" systems that suspend insulin delivery when glucose levels drop dangerously low. The most advanced systems, known as closed-loop insulin delivery systems or "artificial pancreas," combine a CGM sensor, insulin pump, and sophisticated algorithms to automatically adjust insulin delivery in response to real-time glucose readings — closely mimicking the function of a healthy pancreas.

Research into novel diabetes treatments is ongoing across multiple fronts. Beta-cell replacement therapies, including pancreas transplantation and islet transplantation, can restore normal insulin secretion in select individuals with Type 1 diabetes but are limited by the shortage of donor organs and the need for lifelong immunosuppression. Stem cell-derived beta cells offer the potential for an unlimited supply of transplantable insulin-producing cells, and clinical trials are now underway. Gene therapy approaches aimed at inducing immune tolerance to prevent autoimmune destruction of beta cells in Type 1 diabetes are also in early stages of investigation. For Type 2 diabetes, novel drug classes such as GLP-1 receptor agonists (including semaglutide, marketed as Ozempic and Wegovy) have shown remarkable efficacy not only in controlling blood glucose but also in promoting significant weight loss, reducing cardiovascular events, and protecting kidney function.

PSYCHOSOCIAL ASPECTS OF DIABETES

Living with diabetes places substantial psychological, emotional, and social burdens on individuals and their families. "Diabetes distress" — a state of emotional burden related to the demands of diabetes self-management — is experienced by up to 45 percent of people with diabetes and is associated with poorer self-care behaviors and glycemic control. Depression affects approximately 15 to 25 percent of people with diabetes — roughly twice the rate seen in the general population — and has a bidirectional relationship with diabetes, as each condition worsens outcomes for the other. Anxiety, eating disorders, and fear of hypoglycemia are also common among people with diabetes.

The demands of diabetes self-management are continuous and relentless: monitoring blood glucose, counting carbohydrates, adjusting insulin doses, managing hypoglycemic episodes, attending medical appointments, and navigating the psychological weight of a chronic condition with potentially life-altering complications. Diabetes education and self-management support programs are essential components of comprehensive diabetes care. These programs equip people with the knowledge, skills, and confidence to manage their condition effectively on a day-to-day basis. Integration of mental health support — including screening for depression and anxiety, referral to mental health services when needed, and provision of peer support networks — is increasingly recognized as integral to high-quality diabetes care.

DIABETES IN SPECIAL POPULATIONS

Certain groups face unique challenges in diabetes prevention and management. Diabetes in children and adolescents is increasingly prevalent due to the rising rates of childhood obesity. Young people with Type 2 diabetes tend to have a more aggressive disease course than adults, with faster deterioration of beta-cell function and earlier onset of complications. Managing diabetes in childhood and adolescence is complicated by developmental factors including rapid growth, hormonal changes of puberty, and the psychosocial challenges of adolescence.

Diabetes in pregnancy — whether pre-existing or gestational — requires particularly careful management due to the risks it poses to both mother and baby. High blood glucose during pregnancy increases the risk of miscarriage, congenital malformations, fetal overgrowth (macrosomia), preeclampsia, cesarean delivery, stillbirth, and neonatal complications. Intensive blood glucose management before and during pregnancy can substantially reduce these risks.

Diabetes in older adults presents specific challenges related to increased risk of hypoglycemia (which can cause falls, fractures, and cognitive impairment), cognitive decline and dementia (which complicates self-management), polypharmacy (the use of multiple medications, increasing the risk of drug interactions), and frailty and functional limitations. Treatment goals and strategies must be individualized for older adults to balance the benefits of glycemic control against the risks of treatment-related adverse effects.

ECONOMICS AND HEALTH SYSTEMS

The economic burden of diabetes extends far beyond direct medical costs. Indirect costs include lost productivity due to absenteeism, presenteeism (reduced performance at work), disability, and premature mortality. In many low- and middle-income countries, out-of-pocket spending on diabetes care — including medications, blood glucose monitoring supplies, and treatment of complications — pushes families into poverty. Access to insulin, a life-saving medication for people with Type 1 diabetes and many with Type 2, remains a major global health equity issue. Despite insulin being invented over 100 years ago, access to affordable, quality insulin remains inadequate in many parts of the world.

Strengthening health systems to provide comprehensive, person-centered diabetes care requires investment in trained healthcare workers, diagnostic and treatment equipment and supplies, supply chains for medications and monitoring supplies, health information systems for tracking outcomes, and models of care that integrate diabetes management with primary health care. Community health workers and nurses can play a critical role in extending the reach of diabetes care in resource-limited settings. Digital health technologies, including mobile health applications, telemedicine, and electronic health records, offer promising opportunities for improving diabetes care delivery and self-management support, particularly in settings with limited access to specialist care.

SOCIAL DETERMINANTS, EQUITY, AND GLOBAL DIABETES POLICY

Diabetes is both a cause and consequence of poverty, inequality, and social disadvantage. People living in poverty face multiple barriers to effective diabetes prevention and management, including limited access to healthy food, safe environments for physical activity, quality healthcare, and affordable medications and monitoring supplies. Low educational attainment reduces health literacy and the ability to navigate complex self-management demands. Racial and ethnic minorities in many countries experience disproportionately high rates of diabetes and worse diabetes outcomes, reflecting structural racism, discrimination, and social disadvantage.

Global health governance for diabetes has strengthened over recent decades through the development of international targets, guidelines, and policy frameworks. The United Nations Political Declarations on Non-Communicable Diseases (2011, 2014, and 2018) set global commitments for governments to reduce premature mortality from non-communicable diseases, including diabetes, by one-third by 2030. The WHO Global Diabetes Compact, launched in 2021, aims to accelerate national and global action to prevent diabetes and ensure that people living with diabetes can access the care they need to effectively manage their condition. The Compact's initial priority is improving access to insulin and basic technologies for diabetes management in low- and middle-income countries.

The COVID-19 pandemic profoundly disrupted diabetes care globally. Lockdowns, healthcare system overloads, and fear of infection led many people with diabetes to delay or forgo routine care and monitoring. Supply chains for medications, insulin, and monitoring supplies were disrupted. Physical activity declined and unhealthy eating patterns increased among many people with diabetes during lockdown periods, worsening glycemic control. People with diabetes experienced higher rates of severe COVID-19 illness and death, driven by the adverse effects of hyperglycemia on immune function and the increased susceptibility to infections associated with diabetes-related complications. The long-term consequences of these pandemic-related disruptions to diabetes care are likely to include increases in diabetes-related complications and deaths in the years ahead.

Addressing the global diabetes epidemic requires a comprehensive, multi-sectoral, whole-of-society approach that encompasses primary prevention through population-wide healthy lifestyle promotion and modification of the food and physical activity environment; secondary prevention through early detection and treatment of prediabetes and early-stage diabetes; tertiary prevention through comprehensive diabetes management to prevent and treat complications; and action on the social and structural determinants that drive diabetes risk and exacerbate diabetes-related inequities. Achieving this will require sustained political will, adequate investment, and innovative models of care that are adapted to the diverse contexts and needs of people with diabetes around the world.
